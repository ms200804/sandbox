# PLAINTIFFS' MOTION TO VACATE ATTORNEY'S CHARGING LIEN OR, IN THE ALTERNATIVE, TO MODIFY AND SUBORDINATE LIEN

## PRELIMINARY STATEMENT

The Court should respectfully vacate the Notice of Charging Lien filed by Plaintiffs' former law firm, Balestriere PLLC d/b/a Balestriere Fariello (the "Balestriere Firm" or "BF") (Dkt. 284), or in the alternative modify and subordinate it so that Plaintiffs can prosecute their case.

Plaintiffs Julia Hubbard and Kayla Goedinghaus are victims of sex trafficking under the Trafficking Victims Protection Act ("TVPA"). They have alleged that they were raped hundreds of times by the Defendants in this action. Their case is in active discovery, with deadlines approaching. And they cannot prosecute it—not because of any action by Defendants, but because BF has maintained a charging lien claiming "at least 37.5% of any sum recovered." This Court has already determined that BF does not possess a contingency-fee interest and is limited to quantum meruit. (Dkt. 329, adopting Dkt. 327.) For two years, BF has refused to quantify its actual claim, refused to provide time records, and refused to subordinate the lien to litigation funding except on terms this Court's ruling forecloses.

Since that ruling, co-counsel has withdrawn, leaving a solo practitioner as sole counsel. Replacement co-counsel and a litigation funder are available and prepared to engage—both contingent on resolution of the lien. Current counsel has been self-funding the case out of pocket. The lien is the sole obstacle.

Plaintiffs ask that the Court bring the lien into conformity with the legal framework it has already applied—by vacating a lien that claims an impermissible contingency interest—or, in the alternative, by modifying and subordinating the lien so that the case can proceed.

## PROCEDURAL HISTORY

This action arises under the TVPA, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1591, 1595. Plaintiffs allege that they were trafficked for sex by the named Defendants over a period of years. The case was originally filed in the Central District of California and transferred to this Court on May 8, 2023.

Plaintiffs retained the Balestriere Firm in June 2022 under a contingency fee engagement agreement that is, by its express terms, "governed and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of New York." (Dkt. 327 at 2; Dkt. 318 at 5 (BF's own plea conceding same).) On February 1, 2024, Plaintiffs terminated BF for cause. (Hubbard Decl. (Dkt. 327-2) ¶ 2.) BF disputes the "for cause" characterization; that issue is not before the Court on this motion. BF moved to withdraw, and the Court granted the withdrawal on February 13, 2024. (Dkt. 277.)

Three weeks later, on March 5, 2024, BF filed its Notice of Charging Lien (Dkt. 284), claiming entitlement to "at least 37.5% of any sum recovered by way of settlement, and on any judgment that may be recovered." The notice cited both New York Judiciary Law § 475-a and Texas common law, notwithstanding BF's own concession that New York law governs the engagement.

In April 2024, BF filed a plea in intervention (Dkt. 318), seeking to join this action as a party. Plaintiffs opposed, arguing that: (1) under governing New York law, BF is limited to quantum meruit and has no contingency-fee interest; (2) the Fifth Circuit's limited intervention doctrine under *Gaines v. Dixie Carriers, Inc.*, 434 F.2d 52 (5th Cir. 1970), does not extend to quantum meruit claims; and (3) BF's charging lien already adequately protects whatever interest it possesses. (Dkt. 327.) The Court denied intervention "for the reasons stated in Plaintiffs' corrected opposition." (Dkt. 329.)

Since that ruling, circumstances have worsened. In August 2024, BF's sole principal filed for personal bankruptcy under Chapter 7. *In re John G. Balestriere*, No. 1:24-bk-11422 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y.). The trustee subsequently abandoned BF's firm entities as having no viable assets. Pravati Capital, a litigation funder, is listed as a creditor with claims reportedly approaching $20 million. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.)

In November 2025, Plaintiffs' co-counsel, Reinhardt Savic Foley LLP, withdrew with the consent of all parties. (Dkt. 431.) On November 5, 2025, the case was referred in full to Magistrate Judge Chestney. (Dkt. 429.) The current scheduling order sets discovery due July 23, 2026, and dispositive motions due August 24, 2026. (Dkt. 436.) Schmidt Law Corporation remains sole counsel.

## STATEMENT OF FACTS

### A. The Lien Is Blocking Plaintiffs' Ability to Prosecute Their Claims.

Since before BF's withdrawal, Plaintiffs' counsel has been working with Legalist, a litigation funder with whom both counsel and BF have a prior working relationship, to secure funding for this case. The funding arrangement is the same type of arrangement that BF itself had previously pursued with this same funder while BF was still counsel of record. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) BF has its own independent relationship with Legalist and has used Legalist's services on other matters. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.)

Litigation funders, as a standard industry practice, require a first-priority lien on any recovery. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) Legalist has confirmed this requirement repeatedly since March 2024. (Schmidt Decl. ¶ 7 (Dkt. 327-1); Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) The funder cannot proceed while a charging lien of indeterminate scope—claiming 37.5% of any recovery—sits in first position on the docket.

In March 2024, BF's principal stated that he would not agree to subordination unless Plaintiffs agreed to pay BF a percentage of any recovery and release all claims against BF. (Hubbard Decl. ¶ 5 (Dkt. 327-2).) The percentage BF demanded is the interest this Court subsequently determined BF does not possess under New York law. (Dkt. 329.)

Co-counsel withdrew in November 2025. (Dkt. 431.) Plaintiffs are now represented by a single attorney operating a solo practice. Current counsel has been self-funding the case, absorbing approximately $20,000 in discovery platform costs alone, in addition to all other litigation expenses. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) Without litigation funding, Plaintiffs cannot retain contract attorneys for document review, cannot engage the experts this case requires, and cannot build the litigation infrastructure necessary to prosecute a multi-defendant trafficking case through the remaining discovery and dispositive motion practice. (Id.)

Replacement co-counsel has been identified and is prepared to engage, contingent on resolution of the lien and establishment of a funding arrangement. Justin S. Nematzadeh of Nematzadeh PLLC—a former Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher attorney whose matters have recovered more than $3.5 billion for litigants, including a core role in the $3 billion *Petrobras* securities settlement—has reviewed this case and stands ready to appear as co-counsel. (Nematzadeh Decl. ¶¶ 2, 4.) Neither co-counsel nor funder will proceed while BF's unquantified lien remains. The lien is the sole obstacle.

### B. BF Has Refused to Quantify Its Quantum Meruit Claim.

The lien on the docket (Dkt. 284) claims "at least 37.5% of any sum recovered." It does not state a dollar amount, an hourly rate, a number of hours worked, or any other metric from which a quantum meruit value could be derived. It is a contingency-fee claim.

Over the past two years, Plaintiffs' counsel has made repeated requests for BF to produce time records, invoices, or any documentation from which BF's actual quantum meruit claim could be assessed. BF has refused. (Dkt. 327 at 10 n.5 (adopted by Court in Dkt. 329); Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) As of the date of this motion, BF has not produced a single time entry, invoice, or billing record. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) Communications with BF's bankruptcy trustee suggest that contemporaneous billing records may not exist or may have been abandoned with other firm assets. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.)

### C. BF's Principal Is Personally Bankrupt and the Firm May Be Defunct.

On August 16, 2024, BF's sole principal filed a voluntary Chapter 7 petition listing no assets. *In re John G. Balestriere*, No. 1:24-bk-11422 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y.). The trustee, Albert Togut of Togut Segal & Segal LLP, subsequently abandoned BF's firm entities—both Balestriere PLLC and Balestriere Fariello—as having no viable assets. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) Pravati Capital is listed as a creditor of the bankruptcy estate with claims reportedly approaching $20 million and has asserted its own lien against BF. (Id.) Pravati has separately claimed entitlement to a percentage of any recovery in this case independent of BF's interest. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.)

The bankruptcy does not extinguish BF's lien as a matter of law. But it raises unresolved questions about who would receive the proceeds if the lien were satisfied—whether BF's bankrupt principal, the abandoned firm entities, or BF's own creditors. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.)

## LEGAL STANDARD

Federal courts possess inherent authority to manage their proceedings, including the power to control their dockets and ensure the orderly prosecution of cases. *Chambers v. NASCO, Inc.*, 501 U.S. 32, 43 (1991). This authority extends to the management of competing claims on litigation proceeds, including attorney liens. The Fifth Circuit has recognized that federal courts may determine lien priority and order the allocation of recovery among competing claimants. *Villanueva v. CNA Ins. Cos.*, 868 F.2d 684, 686–87 (5th Cir. 1989) (ordering priority among attorney fees and employer's compensation lien at distribution); *Speaks v. Trikora Lloyd P.T.*, 838 F.2d 1436, 1440 (5th Cir. 1988) (same).

Under New York law, which governs the engagement agreement and which this Court has applied (Dkt. 329), a charging lien under Judiciary Law § 475 is equitable in nature and subject to equitable limitations. *Schneider, Kleinick, Weitz, Damashek & Shoot v. City of New York*, 302 A.D.2d 183, 187–88 (1st Dept. 2002) (enforcement of a charging lien is "founded upon the equitable notion that the proceeds of a settlement are ultimately under the control of the court ... and the court will see that no injustice is done to its own officers"); *Rosenman & Colin v. Richard*, 850 F.2d 57, 60 (2d Cir. 1988). The priority of a § 475 lien is not absolute; it "depend[s] on the equities" and is "limited to outcomes where 'proceeds' have been obtained in a client's favor." *Banque Indosuez v. Sopwith Holdings Corp.*, 98 N.Y.2d 34, 43–44 (2002). An attorney discharged from a contingency fee engagement—even without cause—is limited to quantum meruit. *In re Cooperman*, 83 N.Y.2d 465, 473 (1994). An attorney terminated for cause forfeits all right to compensation. *Lansky v. Easow*, 304 A.D.2d 533 (2d Dept. 2003); *Shalom Toy v. NYPIUA*, 239 A.D.2d 196, 198 (1st Dept. 1997).

## ARGUMENT

### I. THE LIEN SHOULD BE VACATED BECAUSE IT CLAIMS AN INTEREST THIS COURT HAS ALREADY DETERMINED BF DOES NOT POSSESS

The lien on its face claims an interest that is inconsistent with the legal framework this Court has already applied.

BF's Notice of Charging Lien (Dkt. 284) claims "at least 37.5% of any sum recovered by way of settlement, and on any judgment that may be recovered." This is a contingency-fee claim. It states no hourly rate, no hours worked, and no dollar amount—only a percentage of recovery.

In denying BF's motion to intervene, the Court adopted the legal analysis in Plaintiffs' opposition, which established that under governing New York law, BF has no contingency-fee interest and is limited to quantum meruit—the reasonable value of services actually rendered. (Dkt. 329; Dkt. 327 at 8–10.) The opposition relied on well-settled authority: *In re Cooperman*, 83 N.Y.2d 465, 473 (1994) (discharged attorney entitled to "reasonable value of services rendered in quantum meruit"); *In re Thelen LLP*, 24 N.Y.3d 16, 28 (2014) ("no law firm has a property interest in future hourly legal fees because they are too contingent in nature and speculative"). The Court also adopted the distinction between a contingency interest (which may support intervention under *Gaines*) and a quantum meruit interest (which does not). *See Sofidiya v. Tex. S. Univ.*, No. 4:19-CV-2221, 2021 WL 4228896, at *5 (S.D. Tex. Apr. 13, 2021).

The lien is therefore facially defective. It purports to secure a 37.5% contingency that is irreconcilable with the governing legal framework. BF has had two years to substitute a quantum meruit figure for the impermissible contingency claim. It has refused. Vacatur is appropriate.

### II. THE LIEN SHOULD BE VACATED OR MODIFIED BECAUSE IT IS OBSTRUCTING PROSECUTION OF PLAINTIFFS' CLAIMS

BF has had a charging lien on this docket for two years. In that time, BF has not quantified its claim, not produced time records, and not agreed to subordination on any terms consistent with this Court's ruling. The lien is no longer serving its protective function. It is preventing Plaintiffs from prosecuting their case.

The Second Circuit has recognized that a discharged attorney's financial interest in a charging lien may diverge from the client's litigation interests, creating a "litigation impasse" or "the subjugation of the client's interests to that of its former counsel." *Butler, Fitzgerald & Potter v. Sequa Corp.*, 250 F.3d 171, 179 (2d Cir. 2001). New York's highest court has confirmed that § 475 lien priority "depend[s] on the equities" and that lien rights are "limited to outcomes where 'proceeds' have been obtained in [a] client's favor." *Banque Indosuez v. Sopwith Holdings Corp.*, 98 N.Y.2d 34, 43–44 (2002). And a § 475 lien may be "waived or forfeited by [the attorney's] misconduct or neglect." *Schneider*, 302 A.D.2d at 188 (citing *Fischer-Hansen v. Brooklyn Heights R.R.*, 173 N.Y. 492, 502 (1903)).

The Fifth Circuit has long recognized federal courts' authority to determine the priority and allocation of competing claims on litigation proceeds—including attorney liens—at the distribution stage. *Villanueva v. CNA Ins. Cos.*, 868 F.2d 684, 686–87 (5th Cir. 1989); *Speaks v. Trikora Lloyd P.T.*, 838 F.2d 1436, 1440 (5th Cir. 1988). If a court may rearrange lien priority when proceeds are distributed, it follows *a fortiori* that a court may address a lien that is preventing the case from reaching distribution at all.

The record here shows a pattern of conduct that is obstructive rather than protective:

*Refusal to quantify.* Despite repeated requests over two years, BF has produced no time records, invoices, or documentation supporting its claimed interest. (Dkt. 327 at 10 n.5 (adopted by Court); Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) The lien claims "at least 37.5%"—not a dollar figure, not a quantum meruit estimate. Nothing.

*Conditioning subordination on impermissible terms.* When asked to subordinate the lien to a standard funding arrangement—the same type of arrangement BF itself previously pursued with the same funder—BF refused unless Plaintiffs agreed to pay BF a percentage of recovery and release all claims. (Hubbard Decl. ¶ 5 (Dkt. 327-2).) That percentage is the interest this Court has determined BF does not possess.

*Resulting prejudice.* Co-counsel has withdrawn. Current counsel is a solo practitioner self-funding a complex multi-defendant TVPA case. The discovery deadline is July 23, 2026. Replacement co-counsel and a litigation funder are available and prepared to engage—contingent on resolution of the lien. Two sex trafficking victims are at risk of being unable to prosecute their claims because a former attorney with a bankrupt principal, no quantified claim, and no time records is maintaining a lien that claims an interest he does not have.

BF's interest is best served by allowing this case to move forward. The TVPA provides for mandatory attorneys' fees to a prevailing plaintiff. 18 U.S.C. § 1595(a). In related litigation involving BF, attorney fee awards have exceeded the underlying verdict. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) If Plaintiffs prevail, there will be ample funds from which to satisfy a reasonable quantum meruit claim. If the lien prevents Plaintiffs from prosecuting their case, the lien itself becomes worthless. Subordination protects BF's interest. The current posture destroys it.[^1]

[^1]: The Court may also consider BF's documented history of fee practices. In *White Lilly, LLC v. Balestriere PLLC*, No. 18-cv-12404 (S.D.N.Y.), an AAA arbitrator found that BF's principal took $1.4 million from client escrow funds in violation of Rule 1.15(a) of the New York Rules of Professional Conduct. The arbitrator ordered complete fee forfeiture. The Southern District confirmed. *See White Lilly*, slip op. at 3–4 (S.D.N.Y. Sep. 21, 2022).

### III. IN THE ALTERNATIVE, THE LIEN SHOULD BE MODIFIED AND SUBORDINATED

If the Court declines to vacate the lien, Plaintiffs request that it be modified and subordinated on terms that resolve the immediate crisis without requiring adjudication of the fee dispute.

*First*, the lien should be modified to strike the claim to "at least 37.5% of any sum recovered." That percentage represents a contingency-fee interest that the Court has already determined BF does not possess under governing New York law. The lien should be limited to BF's actual quantum meruit interest.

*Second*, BF should be required to substantiate its quantum meruit claim by producing contemporaneous time records, invoices, or other documentation within thirty (30) days of the Court's order. If BF cannot or will not substantiate its claim, the lien should be vacated. A lien that its holder cannot quantify after two years does not serve the lien's purpose.

*Third*, any remaining lien should be subordinated to: (a) the litigation funder's first-priority interest, consistent with standard funding practice and with the same funding structure BF itself previously pursued; and (b) any fee arrangement with replacement co-counsel. BF would retain its quantum meruit–limited lien on any net recovery after satisfaction of the funder's and co-counsel's interests.

This structure does not require the Court to determine what BF is owed. It requires only that the lien reflect BF's actual legal entitlement, that BF show a factual basis for its claim, and that the case be permitted to proceed. BF's quantum meruit interest remains protected—it is no longer permitted to prevent the prosecution of the case from which that interest derives. If a recovery is obtained, BF's claim can be resolved through negotiation or, if necessary, in a separate proceeding.

### IV. PLAINTIFFS RESERVE THEIR POSITION THAT BF WAS TERMINATED FOR CAUSE AND IS ENTITLED TO NO COMPENSATION

The Court need not resolve the for-cause question to grant the relief sought here. Plaintiffs preserve their position in full.

Under New York law, an attorney terminated for cause has "no right to compensation." *Shalom Toy*, 239 A.D.2d at 198 (citing *Campagnola v. Mulholland, Minion & Roe*, 76 N.Y.2d 38, 44; *Teichner v. W & J Holsteins*, 64 N.Y.2d 977, 979); *see also Lansky*, 304 A.D.2d at 533 (lien "automatically forfeited" where attorney withdraws without good cause). Plaintiff Hubbard has stated under penalty of perjury that she terminated BF for cause, citing failures in handling child custody matters and service of process. (Hubbard Decl. ¶¶ 2–4 (Dkt. 327-2); Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __, Ex. __ (March 8, 2024 email).) BF disputes the characterization. If it must be resolved, Plaintiffs will seek a hearing.

Even assuming arguendo that the termination was without cause, BF is limited to quantum meruit, and the arguments in Sections I through III establish that the lien is improper and obstructive on that basis alone.

## CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, the Court should respectfully enter an order:

(1) Vacating the Notice of Charging Lien (Dkt. 284) in its entirety; or, in the alternative,

(2) Modifying the lien to: (a) strike the claim to 37.5% of any recovery; (b) require BF to substantiate its quantum meruit claim with contemporaneous time records within thirty (30) days or face vacatur; and (c) subordinate any remaining lien to litigation funding and replacement co-counsel fee arrangements; and

(3) Granting such other and further relief as the Court deems just and proper.

Plaintiffs request a hearing on this motion at the Court's earliest convenience.
