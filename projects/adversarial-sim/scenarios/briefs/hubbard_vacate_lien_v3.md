# PLAINTIFFS' MOTION TO MODIFY AND SUBORDINATE ATTORNEY'S CHARGING LIEN OR, IN THE ALTERNATIVE, TO VACATE LIEN

## PRELIMINARY STATEMENT

Plaintiffs Julia Hubbard and Kayla Goedinghaus are victims of sex trafficking under the Trafficking Victims Protection Act ("TVPA"). Their case is in active discovery, with deadlines approaching. They cannot prosecute it—not because of any action by Defendants, but because their former law firm, Balestriere PLLC d/b/a Balestriere Fariello ("BF"), has maintained an unquantified charging lien on the docket for two years.

BF's lien claims "at least 37.5% of any sum recovered"—a contingency-fee interest that is irreconcilable with the legal framework this Court applied in denying BF's motion to intervene. (Dkt. 329, adopting Dkt. 327.) For two years, BF has refused to quantify its actual quantum meruit claim, refused to produce time records, and refused to subordinate the lien to litigation funding except on terms the Court's framework forecloses. BF's sole principal has since declared personal bankruptcy. The firm entities have been abandoned by the trustee as having no viable assets.

Co-counsel has withdrawn, leaving a solo practitioner self-funding this case. Replacement co-counsel and a litigation funder are prepared to engage—both contingent on resolution of the lien. Without relief, the case will not survive its current deadlines. The lien is the sole obstacle.

Plaintiffs do not ask the Court to adjudicate the underlying fee dispute. They ask the Court to modify and subordinate BF's lien to conform to the legal framework already applied, to require BF to substantiate its quantum meruit claim, and to permit the case to proceed. In the alternative, if the lien cannot be brought into conformity, Plaintiffs ask that it be vacated.

## PROCEDURAL HISTORY

This action arises under the TVPA, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1591, 1595. Plaintiffs allege that they were trafficked for sex by the named Defendants over a period of years. The case was originally filed in the Central District of California and transferred to this Court on May 8, 2023.

Plaintiffs retained the Balestriere Firm in June 2022 under a contingency fee engagement agreement that is, by its express terms, "governed and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of New York." (Dkt. 327 at 2; Dkt. 318 at 5 (BF's own plea conceding same).) On February 1, 2024, Plaintiffs terminated BF for cause. (Hubbard Decl. (Dkt. 327-2) ¶ 2.) BF disputes the "for cause" characterization; that issue is not before the Court on this motion. BF moved to withdraw, and the Court granted the withdrawal on February 13, 2024. (Dkt. 277.)

Three weeks later, on March 5, 2024, BF filed its Notice of Charging Lien (Dkt. 284), claiming entitlement to "at least 37.5% of any sum recovered by way of settlement, and on any judgment that may be recovered." The notice cited both New York Judiciary Law § 475-a and Texas common law, notwithstanding BF's own concession that New York law governs the engagement.

In April 2024, BF filed a plea in intervention (Dkt. 318), seeking to join this action as a party. Plaintiffs opposed, arguing that: (1) under governing New York law, BF is limited to quantum meruit and has no contingency-fee interest; (2) the Fifth Circuit's limited intervention doctrine under *Gaines v. Dixie Carriers, Inc.*, 434 F.2d 52 (5th Cir. 1970), does not extend to quantum meruit claims; and (3) BF's charging lien already adequately protects whatever interest it possesses. (Dkt. 327.) The Court denied intervention "for the reasons stated in Plaintiffs' corrected opposition." (Dkt. 329.)

Since that ruling, circumstances have worsened. In August 2024, BF's sole principal filed for personal bankruptcy under Chapter 7. *In re John G. Balestriere*, No. 1:24-bk-11422 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y.). The trustee subsequently abandoned BF's firm entities as having no viable assets. Pravati Capital, a litigation funder, is listed as a creditor with claims reportedly approaching $20 million. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.)

In November 2025, Plaintiffs' co-counsel, Reinhardt Savic Foley LLP, withdrew with the consent of all parties. (Dkt. 431.) On November 5, 2025, the case was referred in full to Magistrate Judge Chestney. (Dkt. 429.) The current scheduling order sets discovery due July 23, 2026, and dispositive motions due August 24, 2026. (Dkt. 436.) Schmidt Law Corporation remains sole counsel.

## STATEMENT OF FACTS

### A. The Lien Is Blocking Plaintiffs' Ability to Prosecute Their Claims.

Since before BF's withdrawal, Plaintiffs' counsel has been working with Legalist, a litigation funder with whom both counsel and BF have a prior working relationship, to secure funding for this case. The funding arrangement is the same type of arrangement that BF itself had previously pursued with this same funder while BF was still counsel of record. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) BF has its own independent relationship with Legalist and has used Legalist's services on other matters. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.)

Litigation funders, as a standard industry practice, require a first-priority lien on any recovery. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) Legalist has confirmed this requirement repeatedly since March 2024. (Schmidt Decl. ¶ 7 (Dkt. 327-1); Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) The funder cannot proceed while a charging lien of indeterminate scope—claiming 37.5% of any recovery—sits in first position on the docket.

In March 2024, BF's principal stated that he would not agree to subordination unless Plaintiffs agreed to pay BF a percentage of any recovery and release all claims against BF. (Hubbard Decl. ¶ 5 (Dkt. 327-2).) The percentage BF demanded is the interest that is irreconcilable with the legal framework this Court subsequently applied. (Dkt. 329.)

Co-counsel withdrew in November 2025. (Dkt. 431.) Plaintiffs are now represented by a single attorney operating a solo practice. Current counsel has been self-funding the case, absorbing approximately $20,000 in discovery platform costs alone, in addition to all other litigation expenses. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) Without litigation funding, Plaintiffs cannot retain contract attorneys for document review, cannot engage the experts this case requires, and cannot build the litigation infrastructure necessary to prosecute a multi-defendant trafficking case through the remaining discovery and dispositive motion practice. (Id.)

Replacement co-counsel has been identified and is prepared to engage, contingent on resolution of the lien and establishment of a funding arrangement. Justin S. Nematzadeh of Nematzadeh PLLC—a former Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher attorney whose matters have recovered more than $3.5 billion for litigants, including a core role in the $3 billion *Petrobras* securities settlement—has reviewed this case and stands ready to appear as co-counsel. (Nematzadeh Decl. ¶¶ 2, 4.) Neither co-counsel nor funder will proceed while BF's unquantified lien remains. The lien is the sole obstacle.

### B. BF Has Refused to Quantify Its Quantum Meruit Claim.

The lien on the docket (Dkt. 284) claims "at least 37.5% of any sum recovered." It does not state a dollar amount, an hourly rate, a number of hours worked, or any other metric from which a quantum meruit value could be derived. It is a contingency-fee claim.

Over the past two years, Plaintiffs' counsel has made repeated requests for BF to produce time records, invoices, or any documentation from which BF's actual quantum meruit claim could be assessed. BF has refused. (Dkt. 327 at 10 n.5 (adopted by Court in Dkt. 329); Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) As of the date of this motion, BF has not produced a single time entry, invoice, or billing record. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) Communications with BF's bankruptcy trustee suggest that contemporaneous billing records may not exist or may have been abandoned with other firm assets. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.)

### C. BF's Principal Is Personally Bankrupt and the Firm May Be Defunct.

On August 16, 2024, BF's sole principal filed a voluntary Chapter 7 petition listing no assets. *In re John G. Balestriere*, No. 1:24-bk-11422 (Bankr. S.D.N.Y.). The trustee, Albert Togut of Togut Segal & Segal LLP, subsequently abandoned BF's firm entities—both Balestriere PLLC and Balestriere Fariello—as having no viable assets. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) Pravati Capital is listed as a creditor of the bankruptcy estate with claims reportedly approaching $20 million and has asserted its own lien against BF. (Id.) Pravati has separately claimed entitlement to a percentage of any recovery in this case independent of BF's interest. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.)

The bankruptcy does not extinguish BF's lien as a matter of law. But it raises unresolved questions about who would receive the proceeds if the lien were satisfied—whether BF's bankrupt principal, the abandoned firm entities, or BF's own creditors. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.)

## MAGISTRATE JUDGE AUTHORITY

This motion is properly before the Magistrate Judge as a non-dispositive pretrial matter under 28 U.S.C. § 636(b)(1)(A). The motion does not adjudicate BF's fee rights or extinguish its interest in any recovery. It asks the Court to exercise its inherent docket-management authority to bring a charging lien into conformity with the legal framework already applied in this case, and to prevent that lien from obstructing the orderly prosecution of Plaintiffs' claims.

Lien modification and subordination are pretrial case management. They do not "dispose of" any claim or defense within the meaning of § 636(b)(1)(A). *Cf. Gomez v. United States*, 490 U.S. 858, 871–72 (1989) (distinguishing ministerial and case-management functions from dispositive adjudications). Even if the Court determines that vacatur would be dispositive, the primary relief sought—modification and subordination—preserves BF's quantum meruit interest and resolves no disputed question of right. The Court may enter that relief directly.

The referral order of November 5, 2025 (Dkt. 429) referred this case to the Magistrate Judge for pretrial management. This motion falls squarely within that referral.

## LEGAL STANDARD

Federal courts possess inherent authority to manage their proceedings, including the power to control their dockets and ensure the orderly prosecution of cases. *Chambers v. NASCO, Inc.*, 501 U.S. 32, 43 (1991). This authority encompasses the power to address a charging lien that is preventing a case from proceeding. *Id.* at 43–44. The Fifth Circuit has recognized that federal courts may determine lien priority and order the allocation of recovery among competing claimants. *Villanueva v. CNA Ins. Cos.*, 868 F.2d 684, 686–87 (5th Cir. 1989); *Speaks v. Trikora Lloyd P.T.*, 838 F.2d 1436, 1440 (5th Cir. 1988).

Under New York law, which governs the engagement agreement and which this Court has applied (Dkt. 329), a charging lien under Judiciary Law § 475 is equitable in nature and subject to equitable limitations. *Schneider, Kleinick, Weitz, Damashek & Shoot v. City of New York*, 302 A.D.2d 183, 187–88 (1st Dept. 2002) (enforcement of a charging lien is "founded upon the equitable notion that the proceeds of a settlement are ultimately under the control of the court ... and the court will see that no injustice is done to its own officers"); *Rosenman & Colin v. Richard*, 850 F.2d 57, 60 (2d Cir. 1988). The priority of a § 475 lien is not absolute; it "depend[s] on the equities" and is "limited to outcomes where 'proceeds' have been obtained in a client's favor." *Banque Indosuez v. Sopwith Holdings Corp.*, 98 N.Y.2d 34, 43–44 (2002). An attorney discharged from a contingency fee engagement—even without cause—is limited to quantum meruit. *In re Cooperman*, 83 N.Y.2d 465, 473 (1994). An attorney terminated for cause forfeits all right to compensation. *Lansky v. Easow*, 304 A.D.2d 533 (2d Dept. 2003); *Shalom Toy v. NYPIUA*, 239 A.D.2d 196, 198 (1st Dept. 1997).

BF itself invoked New York Judiciary Law § 475-a in its Notice of Charging Lien (Dkt. 284). Having elected to file its lien under New York law, BF cannot now pivot to Texas common-law lien doctrine to avoid the limitations that New York law imposes on a discharged attorney's recovery. *See New Hampshire v. Maine*, 532 U.S. 742, 749–50 (2001) (judicial estoppel prevents a party from asserting a position inconsistent with one successfully advanced in prior proceedings).

## ARGUMENT

### I. THE LIEN IS OBSTRUCTING PROSECUTION OF PLAINTIFFS' CLAIMS AND SHOULD BE MODIFIED OR VACATED

BF has had a charging lien on this docket for two years. In that time, BF has not quantified its claim, not produced time records, and not agreed to subordination on any terms consistent with this Court's framework. The lien is no longer serving its protective function. It is preventing Plaintiffs from prosecuting their case.

The Second Circuit has recognized that a discharged attorney's financial interest in a charging lien may diverge from the client's litigation interests, creating a "litigation impasse" or "the subjugation of the client's interests to that of its former counsel." *Butler, Fitzgerald & Potter v. Sequa Corp.*, 250 F.3d 171, 179 (2d Cir. 2001). New York's highest court has confirmed that § 475 lien priority "depend[s] on the equities" and that lien rights are "limited to outcomes where 'proceeds' have been obtained in [a] client's favor." *Banque Indosuez*, 98 N.Y.2d at 43–44. And a § 475 lien may be "waived or forfeited by [the attorney's] misconduct or neglect." *Schneider*, 302 A.D.2d at 188 (citing *Fischer-Hansen v. Brooklyn Heights R.R.*, 173 N.Y. 492, 502 (1903)).

The Court's inherent authority to manage its docket and ensure the orderly prosecution of cases encompasses the power to address a charging lien that is preventing a case from proceeding. *Chambers*, 501 U.S. at 43–44. This authority exists independent of the New York lien statute and independent of the Court's power to allocate proceeds at distribution. *See Villanueva*, 868 F.2d at 686–87; *Speaks*, 838 F.2d at 1440. If a court may rearrange lien priority when proceeds are distributed, it follows *a fortiori* that a court may address a lien that is preventing the case from reaching distribution at all.

The record here shows a pattern of conduct that is obstructive rather than protective:

*Refusal to quantify.* Despite repeated requests over two years, BF has produced no time records, invoices, or documentation supporting its claimed interest. (Dkt. 327 at 10 n.5 (adopted by Court); Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) The lien claims "at least 37.5%"—not a dollar figure, not a quantum meruit estimate. Nothing.

*Conditioning subordination on impermissible terms.* When asked to subordinate the lien to a standard funding arrangement—the same type of arrangement BF itself previously pursued with the same funder—BF refused unless Plaintiffs agreed to pay BF a percentage of recovery and release all claims. (Hubbard Decl. ¶ 5 (Dkt. 327-2).) That percentage is the interest that is irreconcilable with the legal framework this Court has applied.

*Resulting prejudice.* Co-counsel has withdrawn. Current counsel is a solo practitioner self-funding a complex multi-defendant TVPA case. The discovery deadline is July 23, 2026. Replacement co-counsel and a litigation funder are available and prepared to engage—contingent on resolution of the lien. Without relief, two sex trafficking victims will be unable to prosecute their claims—not because of any action by Defendants, but because a former attorney with a bankrupt principal, no quantified claim, and no time records is maintaining a lien that claims an interest irreconcilable with the framework this Court has applied.

BF's interest is best served by allowing this case to move forward. The TVPA provides for mandatory attorneys' fees to a prevailing plaintiff. 18 U.S.C. § 1595(a). Congress enacted this fee-shifting provision precisely to ensure that trafficking victims can retain counsel and vindicate their rights. If Plaintiffs prevail, there will be ample funds from which to satisfy a reasonable quantum meruit claim—including the statutory fee award. If the lien prevents Plaintiffs from prosecuting their case, the lien itself becomes worthless. Subordination protects BF's interest. The current posture destroys it.

This motion does not ask the Court to adjudicate BF's fee dispute or determine the amount BF is owed. It asks only that BF's lien conform to the legal framework this Court has already applied, and that the lien not be permitted to prevent Plaintiffs from prosecuting their case. BF's quantum meruit interest is preserved under any outcome. Plaintiffs have served this motion on BF's principal, the Chapter 7 trustee, and Pravati Capital to ensure all interested parties have notice and an opportunity to be heard. Plaintiffs request a hearing at which any interested party may appear.

### II. THE LIEN IS FACIALLY INCONSISTENT WITH THE LEGAL FRAMEWORK THIS COURT HAS APPLIED

The lien on its face claims an interest that is inconsistent with the legal framework this Court has applied.

BF's Notice of Charging Lien (Dkt. 284) claims "at least 37.5% of any sum recovered by way of settlement, and on any judgment that may be recovered." This is a contingency-fee claim. It states no hourly rate, no hours worked, and no dollar amount—only a percentage of recovery.

In denying BF's motion to intervene, the Court adopted the legal analysis in Plaintiffs' opposition, which applied the framework under which BF has no contingency-fee interest and is limited to quantum meruit—the reasonable value of services actually rendered. (Dkt. 329; Dkt. 327 at 8–10.) The opposition relied on well-settled authority: *In re Cooperman*, 83 N.Y.2d 465, 473 (1994) (discharged attorney entitled to "reasonable value of services rendered in quantum meruit"); *Matter of Lai Ling Cheng v. Modansky Leasing Co.*, 73 N.Y.2d 454 (1989) (establishing the quantum meruit rule for discharged contingency-fee attorneys). The Court applied the distinction between a contingency interest (which may support intervention under *Gaines*) and a quantum meruit interest (which does not). *See Sofidiya v. Tex. S. Univ.*, No. 4:19-CV-2221, 2021 WL 4228896, at *5 (S.D. Tex. Apr. 13, 2021).

The lien is facially inconsistent with this framework. It purports to secure a 37.5% contingency that is irreconcilable with the quantum meruit limitation this Court has applied. BF has had two years to substitute a quantum meruit figure for the impermissible contingency claim. It has refused.

### III. THE COURT SHOULD MODIFY AND SUBORDINATE THE LIEN

Plaintiffs request that the lien be modified and subordinated on terms that resolve the immediate crisis without requiring adjudication of the fee dispute. This is the most proportionate remedy and the one most consistent with BF's own interests. It preserves BF's quantum meruit claim, requires only that BF substantiate an interest it has had two years to quantify, and permits the case to proceed to a recovery from which BF can be paid.

*First*, the lien should be modified to strike the claim to "at least 37.5% of any sum recovered." That percentage represents a contingency-fee interest that is irreconcilable with the legal framework this Court has applied. The lien should be limited to BF's actual quantum meruit interest.

*Second*, BF should be required to substantiate its quantum meruit claim by producing contemporaneous time records, invoices, or other documentation within thirty (30) days of the Court's order. If BF cannot or will not substantiate its claim, the lien should be vacated. A lien that its holder cannot quantify after two years does not serve the lien's purpose.

*Third*, any remaining lien should be subordinated to: (a) the litigation funder's first-priority interest, consistent with standard funding practice and with the same funding structure BF itself previously pursued; and (b) any fee arrangement with replacement co-counsel. BF would retain its quantum meruit–limited lien on any net recovery after satisfaction of the funder's and co-counsel's interests.

This structure does not require the Court to determine what BF is owed. It requires only that the lien reflect BF's actual legal entitlement, that BF show a factual basis for its claim, and that the case be permitted to proceed. BF's quantum meruit interest remains protected—it is no longer permitted to prevent the prosecution of the case from which that interest derives. If a recovery is obtained, BF's claim can be resolved through negotiation or, if necessary, in a separate proceeding.

If BF fails to comply with the Court's modification order—by failing to produce time records within thirty days, or by failing to substitute a quantum meruit figure for the impermissible contingency claim—vacatur is the appropriate consequence. A lienholder who will not quantify its claim after two years and a court order has forfeited the equitable protection the lien provides. *Schneider*, 302 A.D.2d at 188.

BF's Chapter 7 case does not bar this relief. The trustee has abandoned BF's firm entities as having no viable assets. (Updated Schmidt Decl. ¶ __.) Upon abandonment, the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. § 362(a) ceases to apply to abandoned property. Moreover, modification and subordination of the lien—as distinct from extinguishment—does not constitute an "act to obtain possession of property of the estate" under § 362(a)(3). The lien remains; its terms are brought into conformity with governing law.

Plaintiffs preserve their position that BF was terminated for cause and is entitled to no compensation under New York law. *Shalom Toy*, 239 A.D.2d at 198; *Lansky*, 304 A.D.2d at 533. The Court need not reach this question to grant the relief requested.

## CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, the Court should enter an order:

(1) Modifying the lien to: (a) strike the claim to 37.5% of any recovery; (b) require BF to substantiate its quantum meruit claim with contemporaneous time records within thirty (30) days or face vacatur; and (c) subordinate any remaining lien to litigation funding and replacement co-counsel fee arrangements; or, in the alternative,

(2) Vacating the Notice of Charging Lien (Dkt. 284) in its entirety; and

(3) Granting such other and further relief as the Court deems just and proper.

Plaintiffs request oral argument on this motion at the Court's earliest convenience.
