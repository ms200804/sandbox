# Appellate Review: Motion to Vacate Attorney's Charging Lien

## Top 3 Weaknesses

1. **Reliance on Second Circuit and New York state authority in a Fifth Circuit court without adequate Erie/choice-of-law bridging** — The brief treats NY lien law and 2d Circuit precedent as directly controlling without systematically addressing why a WDTx court should apply these authorities over Texas lien law. The choice-of-law analysis for the *fee agreement* (Dkt. 329) does not automatically extend to the *lien remedy*, which is arguably procedural and governed by forum law. — **Severity: serious**

2. **"Law of the case" overreach regarding the intervention ruling** — The brief treats the Court's denial of intervention (Dkt. 329 adopting Dkt. 327) as a definitive ruling that BF "has no contingency interest and is limited to quantum meruit." But the Court adopted Plaintiffs' opposition to *intervention* — a ruling on the *Gaines* standard for intervention, not a plenary adjudication of BF's fee rights. BF will argue this was dictum or limited to the intervention context, not a binding determination of its substantive lien rights. An appellate court reviewing this would likely agree the intervention denial is not res judicata on the fee question. — **Severity: serious**

3. **Magistrate Judge authority to vacate a lien as a dispositive vs. non-dispositive matter** — The brief does not address whether vacating an attorney's charging lien is a "dispositive" matter under 28 U.S.C. § 636(b)(1), which would require the Magistrate Judge to issue a report and recommendation rather than a final order (absent consent). If BF argues vacatur extinguishes a property right, this could be characterized as dispositive, limiting the Magistrate's authority to an R&R. The brief's silence on this is a gap the opposition will exploit. — **Severity: serious**

## Strongest Single Concern

**The "law of the case" framing of the intervention ruling is the most likely point of reversal.** The entire architecture of the brief — particularly Section I — rests on the premise that the Court has *already determined* BF is limited to quantum meruit and has no contingency interest. But the procedural posture of that ruling was a motion to intervene under *Gaines*, not an adversary proceeding to adjudicate BF's fee rights. The Court adopted Plaintiffs' opposition *for purposes of denying intervention*. An appellate court (or even the Magistrate Judge) could reasonably conclude that this ruling does not preclude BF from asserting its lien rights in a proper proceeding, and that vacating the lien based on this ruling effectively converts an intervention denial into a fee forfeiture without due process. This is the doctrinal argument most likely to give the court pause and most likely to succeed on appeal or objection to the Magistrate's order.

## Full Analysis

### I. Choice-of-Law / Erie Problem

The brief's central premise is that NY law governs, and therefore BF is limited to quantum meruit under *Cooperman* and *Thelen*. The choice-of-law concession (Dkt. 329) covers the *fee agreement*. But the brief conflates three distinct questions:

1. **What law governs the fee agreement?** — NY law (settled by Dkt. 329).
2. **What law governs the charging lien remedy?** — Potentially forum law. A charging lien is an *equitable remedy* created by statute (NY Jud. Law § 475) or common law (Texas). In a federal diversity-like context, the Erie question is whether the lien is substantive (state law applies) or procedural (forum/federal law applies). Texas has its own common-law charging lien doctrine. BF could argue Texas lien law applies to the *lien itself* even if NY law governs the *fee entitlement*.
3. **What law governs the court's power to vacate or subordinate the lien?** — Federal inherent authority (*Chambers*) and Fifth Circuit precedent (*Villanueva*, *Speaks*).

The brief handles question 1 well, jumps to question 3, and largely ignores question 2. This is a gap. The fix is not to abandon the NY-law argument but to add a paragraph explicitly addressing why NY lien law (§ 475) applies to the lien remedy itself — the engagement agreement's choice-of-law clause, BF's own invocation of § 475-a in its lien notice (Dkt. 284), and the Court's adoption of this framework. BF cited § 475-a in its own notice; it should be judicially estopped from pivoting to Texas lien law now.

**Specific fix:** Add a choice-of-law paragraph at the beginning of the Legal Standard section or as a threshold argument, explicitly noting that BF itself invoked NY Jud. Law § 475-a in its lien notice. This forecloses the Texas-law pivot.

### II. "Law of the Case" Overreach

The brief's Section I repeatedly characterizes the intervention denial as a determination that "BF does not possess a contingency-fee interest and is limited to quantum meruit." Citations at pages referencing Dkt. 329 and 327.

The problem: a ruling denying intervention under *Gaines* is procedurally distinct from a ruling adjudicating fee rights. The *Gaines* standard asks whether the intervenor has a "direct, substantial, legally protectable interest." The Court found BF's quantum meruit interest insufficient to meet this standard — but that's a holding about the *Gaines* threshold, not a plenary ruling on BF's substantive rights.

On appeal or objection, BF will argue:
- The intervention ruling is not res judicata on fee rights because it was not a final adjudication on the merits of those rights.
- Due process requires an adversary proceeding before extinguishing a property interest (the lien).
- The intervention denial actually *assumed* BF had a quantum meruit interest — it just found that interest insufficient for intervention. That's not the same as determining BF has *no* contingency interest as a matter of substantive law.

**Doctrinal risk:** The brief's language ("the Court has already determined") is stronger than the procedural posture supports. A more careful formulation would be: "The Court has already applied a legal framework under which BF is limited to quantum meruit" or "The Court adopted analysis establishing that, under governing NY law, a discharged attorney's interest is limited to quantum meruit." This frames the ruling as establishing the *legal framework* rather than adjudicating BF's specific rights — a distinction that is more defensible on appeal.

**Specific fixes:**
- Throughout, replace "the Court has determined that BF does not possess a contingency-fee interest" with "the Court has applied a legal framework under which BF's interest is limited to quantum meruit."
- In Section I, add a sentence acknowledging that this motion does not require re-adjudication of BF's fee rights — it asks only that the lien *on the docket* conform to the legal framework the Court has already adopted.
- Consider adding a due process paragraph: Plaintiffs are not seeking to extinguish BF's claim; they are seeking to bring the lien notice into conformity with governing law and to prevent it from obstructing prosecution. BF retains the right to pursue its quantum meruit claim in a separate proceeding.

### III. Magistrate Judge Authority — Dispositive/Non-Dispositive

Under 28 U.S.C. § 636(b)(1)(A), a Magistrate Judge may determine non-dispositive pretrial matters. Dispositive matters (listed in § 636(b)(1)(B)) require an R&R. The brief states the case was "referred in full" to the Magistrate Judge (Dkt. 429) but does not specify whether this is a consent referral under § 636(c) or a pretrial referral under § 636(b).

The scenario states "pretrial referral only, not full consent referral." This matters because:

- If vacating the lien is **dispositive** (extinguishing a property right), the Magistrate can only issue an R&R, reviewed de novo by the District Judge.
- If vacating the lien is **non-dispositive** (docket management, pretrial order), the Magistrate can rule directly, reviewed for clear error.

The brief should address this proactively. The strongest framing: this motion is a *pretrial management* order — controlling the docket, ensuring orderly prosecution, and bringing a lien notice into conformity with the Court's prior ruling. It does not adjudicate BF's underlying fee claim. This is non-dispositive. Alternatively, even if dispositive, the Magistrate can issue an R&R.

**Specific fix:** Add a brief procedural section (or footnote) establishing that this relief is within the Magistrate Judge's authority as a non-dispositive pretrial matter. Cite *Gomez v. United States*, 490 U.S. 858 (1989), and relevant Fifth Circuit authority on the dispositive/non-dispositive distinction.

### IV. Circuit Authority Mismatch

The brief's substantive authorities are overwhelmingly from the Second Circuit and New York state courts: *Butler, Fitzgerald* (2d Cir.), *Banque Indosuez* (N.Y. Ct. App.), *Schneider* (1st Dept.), *Cooperman* (N.Y. Ct. App.), *Thelen* (N.Y. Ct. App.), *Rosenman & Colin* (2d Cir.).

The Fifth Circuit authorities are limited to *Villanueva* and *Speaks* (both on lien priority at distribution) and *Chambers* (Supreme Court, inherent authority). None directly address vacatur of a charging lien pre-judgment.

An appellate court in the Fifth Circuit would want to see:
- Whether the Fifth Circuit has addressed vacatur (as opposed to priority) of attorney liens.
- Whether WDTx or other Texas federal courts have applied NY § 475 lien law.
- Whether there is any tension between the NY authorities and Texas/Fifth Circuit lien doctrine.

**Doctrinal note:** The *a fortiori* argument in Section II ("if a court may rearrange lien priority when proceeds are distributed, it follows *a fortiori* that a court may address a lien that is preventing the case from reaching distribution at all") is rhetorically effective but doctrinally unsupported. *Villanueva* and *Speaks* addressed lien *priority* among competing claimants at the *distribution* stage — they did not address pre-judgment *vacatur* of a lien. The leap from "may determine priority" to "may vacate entirely" is significant and unaddressed. BF will argue these are categorically different remedies.

**Specific fix:** Find Fifth Circuit or WDTx authority on inherent authority to manage attorney liens pre-judgment. If none exists, frame the *a fortiori* argument more carefully and ground it in *Chambers* inherent authority rather than *Villanueva*/*Speaks*.

### V. The *Cooperman* / Quantum Meruit Framework

The citation to *In re Cooperman*, 83 N.Y.2d 465, 473 (1994) is correct but worth examining closely. *Cooperman* holds that a discharged attorney in a contingency case is limited to quantum meruit. However, *Cooperman* was primarily about *non-refundable retainer fees* and the ethics of fee forfeiture clauses — its quantum meruit holding is established but its facts are distinguishable.

More directly on point would be *Matter of Lai Ling Cheng v. Modansky Leasing Co.*, 73 N.Y.2d 454 (1989), which established the quantum meruit rule for discharged contingency-fee attorneys. The brief cites *Cooperman* but not *Lai Ling Cheng*. This is a minor gap but could be flagged by a careful court.

*In re Thelen LLP*, 24 N.Y.3d 16 (2014), is cited for the proposition that "no law firm has a property interest in future hourly legal fees." This is accurate but *Thelen* involved a dissolved firm's right to fees from pending matters — a partnership dissolution context, not a discharged-attorney context. The holding is analogous but the factual setting is different. An appellate court might view this citation as somewhat strained.

**Severity: minor.** The core quantum meruit principle is well-established under NY law. The citations support the proposition even if they aren't the most precisely on-point authorities.

### VI. Due Process / Property Interest Concern

This is the appellate issue most likely to generate briefing from BF: whether vacating an attorney's charging lien without an evidentiary hearing violates due process.

A § 475 charging lien, once it attaches, creates a property interest. *See LMWT Realty Corp. v. Davis Agency Inc.*, 85 N.Y.2d 462, 467 (1995). Vacating it without a hearing or adversary proceeding could be challenged as a deprivation of property without due process. The brief's alternative relief (modify and subordinate, with 30-day substantiation requirement) is more defensible because it preserves BF's interest while imposing reasonable conditions.

**Strategic recommendation:** The brief should lean harder on the alternative relief (Section III) as the primary ask, with vacatur as the stretch position. As currently structured, the brief leads with vacatur and treats modification/subordination as fallback. Appellate resilience improves if the brief presents modification/subordination as the reasonable, proportionate remedy and vacatur as appropriate only if BF fails to comply (i.e., cannot substantiate within 30 days).

### VII. Standard of Review on Appeal

If the Magistrate Judge issues an order (non-dispositive), review by the District Judge is for clear error or contrary to law. 28 U.S.C. § 636(b)(1)(A); Fed. R. Civ. P. 72(a). This is a deferential standard.

If the Magistrate issues an R&R (dispositive), review is de novo. § 636(b)(1)(C); Fed. R. Civ. P. 72(b).

On appeal to the Fifth Circuit, the standard depends on the nature of the ruling:
- Legal questions (choice of law, scope of § 475): **de novo**.
- Equitable determinations (balancing equities, lien modification): **abuse of discretion**.
- Factual findings: **clearly erroneous**.

The brief's strongest positions are the equitable arguments (Sections II and III), which receive deferential review. Its weakest positions are the legal arguments (Section I — "law of the case" overreach), which receive de novo review. This is a problem because the brief leads with its most appellate-vulnerable argument.

### VIII. Specific Line-Level Issues

**Preliminary Statement, ¶1:** "This Court has already determined that BF does not possess a contingency-fee interest" — overstates the intervention ruling. Revise as discussed above.

**Procedural History, ¶4:** "The Court denied intervention 'for the reasons stated in Plaintiffs' corrected opposition.'" — This one-line adoption is both a strength (the Court agreed) and a weakness (there's no independent judicial reasoning to cite). Note that on appeal, the absence of independent judicial analysis makes the ruling more vulnerable to being characterized as summary.

**Section I, heading:** "THE LIEN SHOULD BE VACATED BECAUSE IT CLAIMS AN INTEREST THIS COURT HAS ALREADY DETERMINED BF DOES NOT POSSESS" — The heading itself overstates. Suggest: "THE LIEN SHOULD BE VACATED BECAUSE IT IS INCONSISTENT WITH THE LEGAL FRAMEWORK THIS COURT HAS APPLIED."

**Section II, ¶ on *a fortiori*:** As discussed, the leap from distribution-stage priority to pre-judgment vacatur is not self-evident. Strengthen or qualify.

**Section II, footnote re *White Lilly*:** Including this is a judgment call. It's relevant to BF's credibility but risks being seen as a character attack. If included, frame it carefully as relevant to the equitable balancing (pattern of fee misconduct goes to whether equity favors protecting BF's claimed interest). An appellate court would likely find it admissible for equitable purposes but could view it as prejudicial if overemphasized.

**Section III, alternative relief:** This is the strongest section doctrinally. The three-part structure (modify, substantiate, subordinate) is clean and proportionate. This should arguably be the lead argument, not the fallback.

**Section IV, reservation of for-cause position:** Well handled. Preserving without litigating is the right call. No appellate issue here.

### IX. Preservation Issues

The brief adequately preserves the following arguments for appeal:
- Inherent authority to vacate/modify liens
- NY law governs, BF limited to quantum meruit
- Equitable balancing under § 475 / *Banque Indosuez*
- For-cause termination (expressly reserved)

The brief does **not** adequately preserve:
- A constitutional due process argument (if BF raises one, Plaintiffs should be prepared to respond)
- The dispositive/non-dispositive distinction (if the Magistrate's authority is challenged)
