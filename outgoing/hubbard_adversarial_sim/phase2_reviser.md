**NEW SECTION: MAGISTRATE JUDGE AUTHORITY**

> This motion is properly before the Magistrate Judge as a non-dispositive pretrial matter under 28 U.S.C. § 636(b)(1)(A). The motion does not adjudicate BF's fee rights or extinguish its interest in any recovery. It asks the Court to exercise its inherent docket-management authority to bring a charging lien into conformity with the legal framework already applied in this case, and to prevent that lien from obstructing the orderly prosecution of Plaintiffs' claims.
>
> Lien modification and subordination are routine pretrial management. They do not "dispose of" any claim or defense within the meaning of § 636(b)(1)(A). *Cf. Gomez v. United States*, 490 U.S. 858, 871–72 (1989) (distinguishing ministerial and case-management functions from dispositive adjudications). Even if the Court determines that vacatur would be dispositive, the alternative relief sought — modification and subordination — preserves BF's quantum meruit interest and resolves no disputed question of right. The Court may enter that relief directly.
>
> The referral order of November 5, 2025 (Dkt. 429) referred this case to the Magistrate Judge for pretrial management. This motion falls squarely within that referral.

## Unfixable Issues

**1. No direct Fifth Circuit authority on pre-judgment lien vacatur or subordination.**
This is a gap in the case law, not a gap in the brief. The argument must be built from *Chambers* inherent authority, the *Villanueva*/*Speaks* analogy (reframed more carefully), and the overwhelming equities. The doctrinal novelty is real, and BF will exploit it. Mitigation: leading with modification/subordination (rather than vacatur) is more defensible as routine docket management. Research should continue — look for WDTx or Fifth Circuit decisions managing attorney liens pretrial, even in unpublished opinions — but proceed on the assumption that direct authority may not exist.

**2. BF will raise a due process objection regardless of framing.**
Even modification affects BF's asserted rights. No amount of careful drafting eliminates this objection. The best mitigation is demonstrating procedural fairness: proper service on all interested parties, requesting a hearing, and framing the relief as preserving (not extinguishing) BF's quantum meruit claim. The brief should make the court comfortable that BF has been heard — or has deliberately chosen not to participate — before ruling.

**3. The Pravati wild card.**
If Pravati Capital asserts an independent claim on recovery (as the Updated Schmidt Declaration apparently indicates), clearing BF's lien may not fully solve the funding problem. The brief should acknowledge this and argue that Pravati's claim against BF is between Pravati and BF's bankruptcy estate — it is not a charge on Plaintiffs' recovery. But this is a fact pattern the court may want to explore, and it could complicate the hearing. The brief cannot control what Pravati does.

**4. The intervention denial is not res judicata on lien validity.**
This is a real doctrinal limitation. Dkt. 329 denied intervention under the *Gaines* standard — it applied a legal framework for that purpose. It is not a plenary adjudication of BF's lien rights, and BF will argue (with some force) that treating it as such converts an intervention denial into a fee forfeiture without due process. The revised brief addresses this by softening the "law of the case" language and reframing, but the underlying tension remains. The court adopted the framework; the brief asks the lien to conform to it. That's defensible but not bulletproof.
