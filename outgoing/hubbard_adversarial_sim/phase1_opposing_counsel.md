# OPPOSING COUNSEL ANALYSIS
## Balestriere PLLC's Perspective — Aggressive Calibration

---

### Top 3 Weaknesses

1. **No adversary proceeding or due process for lien adjudication** — Plaintiffs are asking a Magistrate Judge on pretrial referral to effectively extinguish a property right without an evidentiary hearing, without BF having been served with this motion as a party (BF was denied intervention), and without the procedural protections of an adversary proceeding. The lien is a chose in action — property — and its summary vacatur raises serious due process concerns. — **Severity: serious**

2. **The Dkt. 329 ruling doesn't say what Plaintiffs claim it says** — The Court denied *intervention*, adopting Plaintiffs' argument that a quantum meruit interest doesn't support intervention under *Gaines*. That is not the same as ruling that the lien itself is invalid or that BF has no enforceable charging lien. Plaintiffs are bootstrapping a narrow procedural ruling (no intervention) into a substantive ruling on BF's lien rights. The Court never adjudicated the lien's validity. — **Severity: serious**

3. **The "obstruction" is manufactured by Plaintiffs' own funder's business requirements** — A litigation funder's demand for first-priority position is a private commercial preference, not an equitable ground for stripping a pre-existing lien. Plaintiffs chose this funder, chose not to seek alternative funding structures, and are now asking the Court to destroy BF's property right to accommodate a third-party investor's terms. The equities don't run that way. — **Severity: serious**

---

### Strongest Single Attack Vector

**The motion is a due process nullity.** BF is not a party to this action — the Court denied intervention. BF has no obligation to respond to a motion filed in a case in which it has no party status. Yet Plaintiffs are asking the Court to adjudicate and extinguish BF's property right — a charging lien — without BF at the table. This is not a scheduling dispute or a case management order; it is a dispositive ruling on a third party's property interest. The Magistrate Judge lacks authority to enter such an order on pretrial referral under 28 U.S.C. § 636(b)(1)(A), because vacating or modifying a lien is dispositive of BF's rights and is analogous to an involuntary dismissal or injunctive relief. At minimum, this requires a report and recommendation to the District Judge. And under basic due process, BF is entitled to notice, an opportunity to be heard, and an evidentiary hearing before its property is taken. Plaintiffs' motion should be denied on this threshold ground alone.

### Full Analysis

#### I. JURISDICTIONAL AND PROCEDURAL ATTACKS

**A. Magistrate Judge Authority — This Is Dispositive**

The case was "referred in full" to Magistrate Judge Chestney (Dkt. 429), but Plaintiffs describe this as a "pretrial referral only, not full consent referral." If that's correct, the Magistrate Judge's authority is limited under § 636(b)(1)(A) to nondispositive pretrial matters. Vacating or modifying a third party's charging lien — which extinguishes or diminishes a property right — is dispositive. It is functionally equivalent to an injunction against BF's enforcement of its lien. Under *Meador v. Tidewater Express Lines, Inc.*, 420 F.2d 694 (5th Cir. 1969), and the weight of authority, only an Article III judge can enter such an order, or the Magistrate Judge must issue a report and recommendation subject to de novo review.

**What I'd file:** A threshold objection that the Magistrate Judge lacks authority to grant this relief, requiring either (a) consent of all affected parties under § 636(c) (which BF, as a non-party, has not given), or (b) a report and recommendation.

**B. Due Process — BF Is Not a Party**

This is the kill shot on the motion's procedural viability. BF was denied intervention. It is not a party. Plaintiffs are now asking the Court to adjudicate BF's property rights in a proceeding to which BF is not a party and in which BF has no procedural rights — no right to discovery, no right to cross-examine, no right to present evidence. The motion relies entirely on declarations from Plaintiffs' counsel and Plaintiffs themselves — adversaries of BF — without any opportunity for BF to respond.

Under *Mathews v. Eldridge*, 424 U.S. 319 (1976), due process requires, at minimum: (1) notice, (2) an opportunity to be heard, and (3) a neutral decisionmaker. BF has been given none of these in a proceeding designed to extinguish its property.

**What I'd file:** A special appearance objecting to the Court's exercise of jurisdiction over BF's property rights in a proceeding to which BF is not a party, and demanding that any adjudication of the lien occur in a proper adversary proceeding — either a separate action or, if in this case, with BF granted intervention for the limited purpose of defending its lien.

*Note the irony:* Plaintiffs successfully argued that BF should not be allowed to intervene (Dkt. 327/329), and now are asking the Court to adjudicate BF's rights without BF present. They want it both ways — BF out of the case when BF wants in, but BF's rights adjudicated when Plaintiffs want them gone.

**C. The Proper Forum Is Bankruptcy Court or a Separate Action**

BF's principal is in Chapter 7. The lien is an asset of the estate (or was, prior to abandonment). If the trustee abandoned the firm entities, the question of who owns the lien — the debtor, the estate, or BF's creditors (including Pravati Capital) — is a bankruptcy question. Plaintiffs acknowledge this: "it raises unresolved questions about who would receive the proceeds if the lien were satisfied." This Court should not be adjudicating a property interest whose ownership is entangled in a pending bankruptcy proceeding in the Southern District of New York.

**What I'd argue:** The lien may be property of the bankruptcy estate under 11 U.S.C. § 541. Even if the trustee abandoned the firm entities, abandonment of an entity is not abandonment of specific assets like a charging lien. The automatic stay may apply, or at minimum the bankruptcy court's jurisdiction over estate property should be respected. This Court should abstain.

#### II. SUBSTANTIVE ATTACKS ON EACH ARGUMENT

**A. Section I — "The Lien Claims an Interest BF Doesn't Possess"**

This is Plaintiffs' strongest argument, and here's how I distinguish it:

The Dkt. 329 order denied *intervention*. It adopted the analysis that BF's quantum meruit interest doesn't support intervention under *Gaines*. But it did **not** rule on the validity of the lien itself. A charging lien under § 475 and a right to intervene are different legal instruments with different standards. Plaintiffs are conflating them.

Moreover, even under quantum meruit, BF retains a charging lien. New York Judiciary Law § 475 grants an attorney a lien "upon [the client's] cause of action... from the commencement of an action." The lien attaches regardless of whether the fee arrangement is contingency or hourly. *LMWT Realty Corp. v. Davis Agency Inc.*, 85 N.Y.2d 462, 467 (1995). The *amount* secured by the lien may be limited to quantum meruit, but the *lien itself* is valid. Plaintiffs' argument that the lien should be vacated because it states "37.5%" rather than a dollar figure confuses the lien's face amount with its legal enforceability. The proper remedy is modification (capping the lien at quantum meruit value), not vacatur.

**Line-level critique of the brief:**

- Page 7, ¶1: "The lien is therefore facially defective." This is an overstatement. A lien that overstates the amount secured is subject to modification, not vacatur. Plaintiffs cite no authority for the proposition that an overstated lien is void rather than voidable.
- The brief cites *In re Thelen LLP* for the proposition that "no law firm has a property interest in future hourly legal fees." But *Thelen* addressed whether a dissolved firm could claim fees for matters in progress at dissolution — a partnership dissolution case. It did not address charging liens. This citation is misleading if used to suggest § 475 liens are unenforceable.

**B. Section II — "The Lien Is Obstructing Prosecution"**

This section is emotionally powerful but legally thin. My attacks:

1. **The obstruction is self-inflicted.** Plaintiffs chose to terminate BF. They chose a funder that demands first priority. They chose not to seek alternative funding. They chose not to bring this motion for two years. The "crisis" is of Plaintiffs' own making. Under *Schneider*, the equities must be considered — and the equities include the fact that BF performed work (however much) that contributed to the case's current posture.

2. **"Two years of refusal" cuts both ways.** BF has no obligation to assist Plaintiffs in subordinating its own lien. Refusing to subordinate is not misconduct — it is the exercise of a legal right. Plaintiffs characterize the refusal as "obstructive," but a lienholder is entitled to protect its position. The fact that BF conditioned subordination on a percentage of recovery may have been aggressive, but it was a negotiating position, not an abuse of process.

3. ***Butler v. Sequa* is Second Circuit authority, not binding here.** The Fifth Circuit has not adopted the "litigation impasse" framework. Plaintiffs are asking a WDTx court to apply a Second Circuit equitable doctrine that has no Fifth Circuit analog. While the choice-of-law issue may bring in NY substantive law on the lien, the procedural question of what remedies are available in federal court is governed by the law of the circuit.

4. **The TVPA mandatory fees argument is a red herring.** Section 1595(a) provides for fees to a *prevailing plaintiff's* attorney. It does not create a fund from which former counsel's quantum meruit claims are satisfied. BF's lien is on the recovery, not on the fee award. Plaintiffs are conflating two different pots of money.

5. **The footnote about *White Lilly* is a cheap shot and potentially inadmissible.** Prior bad acts from an unrelated case, presented in a footnote, are prejudicial and irrelevant to the legal question of whether *this* lien should be vacated. I'd move to strike it. If Plaintiffs want to argue for-cause termination (which they expressly reserve for later), they can brief that separately. Smuggling it into a footnote is advocacy by innuendo.

**C. Section III — Alternative Relief (Modification and Subordination)**

This is actually Plaintiffs' most reasonable argument, and as opposing counsel, I'd concede some ground here to avoid vacatur — but on different terms:

- **The 30-day deadline for time records is arbitrary and punitive.** BF's principal is in bankruptcy. The firm's records may be in the possession of the trustee or may have been abandoned. Demanding production within 30 days — with vacatur as the penalty — is designed to be unachievable, not reasonable.

- **Subordination to what?** Plaintiffs want subordination to both a litigation funder *and* replacement co-counsel. They don't specify the funder's terms, the percentage, the co-counsel fee arrangement, or any cap. BF is being asked to subordinate to a blank check. The Court should not order subordination without knowing what BF is being subordinated to.

- **My counter-proposal:** I'd offer subordination — but only to litigation costs (not the funder's return on investment), with BF retaining priority over the funder's profit component, and with a cap on total deductions before BF's quantum meruit claim is satisfied. I'd also demand that any subordination order preserve BF's right to a hearing on quantum meruit value before any distribution.

**D. Section IV — "For Cause" Reservation**

This section is a litigation threat disguised as a reservation. It serves no purpose in this motion except to prejudice the Court against BF. If Plaintiffs aren't seeking a for-cause determination, this section should be stricken. If they are, they should file a separate motion. Including it here — with the citation to *Shalom Toy* (automatic forfeiture) and the Hubbard Declaration allegations — is designed to make the Court believe BF may deserve nothing, without giving BF an opportunity to respond to the for-cause allegations.

**What I'd file:** A motion to strike Section IV and the *White Lilly* footnote as irrelevant, prejudicial, and outside the scope of the relief sought.

#### III. DISCOVERY AND MOTION PRACTICE I'D PURSUE

1. **Motion to dismiss/deny the motion** on procedural grounds (no party status, no due process, Magistrate Judge authority).
2. **Motion to strike** Section IV and the *White Lilly* footnote.
3. **If forced to engage on the merits:** Cross-motion for an evidentiary hearing on quantum meruit value, with discovery into (a) BF's actual work product and hours, (b) the value of BF's contribution to the case, (c) the terms of the proposed funding arrangement, and (d) whether Plaintiffs' termination of BF was actually for cause (since they raised it).
4. **Opposition to subordination** without full disclosure of the funder's terms and co-counsel's fee arrangement.
5. **Intervention motion (renewed)** — for the limited purpose of defending the lien. If the Court is going to adjudicate the lien, BF must be at the table. The denial of general intervention under *Gaines* doesn't foreclose limited intervention to defend a property interest under Fed. R. Civ. P. 24(a)(2).

#### IV. NARRATIVE FOR THE COURT

Here's the story I'd tell the judge:

*BF represented these plaintiffs for nearly two years in a complex trafficking case. BF built the case — took the depositions, drafted the complaints, navigated transfer, and invested substantial time and resources on a contingency basis. Plaintiffs then fired BF, retained new counsel, and now want the Court to erase BF's interest so that a litigation funder — a for-profit investor — can take first position on any recovery. This isn't about justice for trafficking victims. It's about money. Specifically, it's about whether a litigation funder's profit takes priority over the lawyers who actually built the case.*

*The lien isn't blocking anything. The lien is a placeholder. Plaintiffs could have moved to modify it two years ago. They didn't. They could have proposed reasonable subordination terms. They didn't. Instead, they waited until co-counsel withdrew and the discovery deadline approached, manufactured a crisis, and now ask this Court to strip BF's property rights in an ex parte proceeding to which BF isn't even a party.*

*That's not equity. That's ambush.*

#### VI. CASE LAW I'D CITE IN OPPOSITION

- *LMWT Realty Corp. v. Davis Agency Inc.*, 85 N.Y.2d 462 (1995) — § 475 lien attaches regardless of fee arrangement type
- *Rosen v. Rosen*, 97 A.D.2d 837 (2d Dept. 1983) — charging lien is a "vested property right"
- *Klein v. Eubank*, 87 N.Y.2d 459 (1996) — attorney's lien survives discharge; cannot be defeated by client's unilateral action
- *Kaplan v. Reuss*, 113 A.D.2d 184 (2d Dept. 1985) — court should not vacate lien without plenary hearing
- *In re San Juan Dupont Plaza Hotel Fire Litig.*, 802 F.2d 9 (1st Cir. 1986) — due process requires notice and hearing before adjudication of lien rights
- *Orix Credit Alliance, Inc. v. Wolfe*, 212 F.3d 891 (5th Cir. 2000) — Fifth Circuit on property rights and due process in federal proceedings

---

**Bottom line:** The brief is well-written and emotionally compelling, but it has a serious structural vulnerability — it's asking a court to take property from a non-party without due process. If I'm BF's counsel, I don't engage on the merits at all. I attack the procedure, demand intervention rights, and force Plaintiffs to either let me in the case or take this to a separate proceeding where I have full rights. On the merits, the strongest position is conceding modification (cap the lien at quantum meruit) but opposing vacatur and fighting subordination terms. The brief's biggest rhetorical weakness is the gap between what Dkt. 329 actually decided (no intervention) and what Plaintiffs claim it decided (no valid lien). An aggressive opposition would hammer that distinction relentlessly.
