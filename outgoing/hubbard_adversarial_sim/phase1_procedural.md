# Procedural Specialist Analysis: Motion to Vacate Attorney's Charging Lien

## Top 3 Weaknesses

1. **Magistrate Judge authority — dispositive vs. non-dispositive classification is unaddressed** — If BF argues vacating a charging lien is a dispositive motion (affecting a property right), the Magistrate Judge can only issue a Report & Recommendation under 28 U.S.C. § 636(b)(1)(B), not a final order. The brief assumes the Magistrate can enter a binding order but never argues why this is non-dispositive. BF will raise this to delay, forcing re-briefing before the District Judge. — **Severity: serious**

2. **No Rule 7(a) conference or Local Rule compliance shown** — W.D. Tex. Local Rules require a certificate of conference (LR CV-7(i)) demonstrating counsel conferred or attempted to confer with opposing counsel before filing a non-discovery motion. The brief contains no such certificate. BF's counsel (or whoever is authorized to act for BF given the bankruptcy) must be identified and conferenced. If BF has no active counsel in this case — it withdrew — the motion may need to be served directly on the bankruptcy trustee or BF's principal. The certificate of service says "all counsel of record via electronic notification," but BF may no longer be a party or have counsel of record, meaning CM/ECF service is insufficient. — **Severity: serious**

3. **No deadline pressure is tied to a specific procedural trigger** — The brief references the July 23, 2026 discovery deadline and says funding/co-counsel are "contingent on resolution of the lien," but it never quantifies how long onboarding co-counsel and the funder will actually take, or identifies which specific discovery obligations are at risk (deposition deadlines, expert disclosures, etc.). Without a concrete timeline showing "if this motion isn't resolved by X, we will miss Y deadline," the court has no procedural urgency to act quickly. A motion for expedited consideration or shortened briefing schedule should accompany this filing. — **Severity: serious**

## Strongest Single Concern

**The dispositive/non-dispositive question before the Magistrate Judge.** This is the procedural issue most likely to derail the motion regardless of its merits. Vacating a charging lien extinguishes what BF will characterize as a vested property right under NY Judiciary Law § 475. If the Magistrate treats this as dispositive, the motion becomes an R&R subject to de novo review — adding months to resolution. If the brief doesn't preemptively argue that this is non-dispositive (a case-management order controlling the docket, not an adjudication of BF's fee entitlement), BF gets a free procedural objection. The brief's alternative relief (modify and subordinate) is more defensible as non-dispositive, which is another reason to lead with that framing rather than outright vacatur.

## Full Analysis

### A. Magistrate Judge Authority (28 U.S.C. § 636)

The brief states the case was "referred in full to Magistrate Judge Chestney" (Dkt. 429) but describes this as a "pretrial referral only, not full consent referral." This distinction is critical and underdeveloped.

Under § 636(b)(1)(A), a Magistrate Judge may determine non-dispositive pretrial matters. Under § 636(b)(1)(B), dispositive motions require an R&R with de novo review by the District Judge. The Eighth Amendment of the Federal Magistrates Act lists dispositive motions (summary judgment, dismissal, involuntary dismissal, suppression) — but the list is not exhaustive.

BF will argue that vacating a charging lien is functionally a summary adjudication of a property right, making it dispositive. The Fifth Circuit hasn't squarely addressed this for attorney liens, but *Retired Chicago Police Ass'n v. City of Chicago*, 76 F.3d 856, 869 (7th Cir. 1996), suggests that orders effectively disposing of a claim are dispositive regardless of their label.

**Recommendation:** Add a section (or at minimum a footnote) arguing this is non-dispositive. The strongest framing: this is a docket-management order under the court's inherent authority, not an adjudication of BF's underlying fee claim. The motion does not determine what BF is owed — it controls how the lien interacts with the case's progression. Cite *Gomez v. United States*, 490 U.S. 858 (1989) (additional duties consistent with Constitution may be referred). Alternatively, if the referral order is broad enough, argue the District Judge delegated authority over all pretrial matters including lien management. Obtain and cite the actual referral order language.

### B. Service and Standing — Who Represents BF?

This is a latent procedural trap the brief doesn't address:

- BF withdrew as counsel of record (Dkt. 277).
- BF's principal is in Chapter 7 bankruptcy.
- The trustee abandoned BF's firm entities.

So who is the proper respondent to this motion? Several possibilities:

1. **BF's principal pro se** — if the lien is personal property of the estate, the trustee has standing; if abandoned, it reverts to the debtor. But if the debtor is discharged, the lien may be estate property that was never administered.
2. **The Chapter 7 trustee** — if the lien wasn't formally abandoned or if the abandonment didn't specifically cover the lien interest in this case.
3. **Pravati Capital** — the brief mentions Pravati "has asserted its own lien" and claimed entitlement to recovery. If Pravati has a security interest in BF's lien, Pravati may be a necessary party under Rule 19.

**Recommendation:** Before filing, determine: (a) whether the Chapter 7 trustee's abandonment order specifically covered BF's interest in this case; (b) whether Pravati has a perfected security interest in the lien proceeds; (c) whether BF's principal has been discharged. If Pravati has an interest, failing to join Pravati risks an incomplete resolution — the court vacates BF's lien only for Pravati to assert its own claim. The brief should address necessary parties under Rule 19(a) or explain why Pravati's claimed interest doesn't require joinder.

Service must go beyond CM/ECF. BF is not a party to this case (intervention was denied). The motion should be served on: BF's principal at a current address, the Chapter 7 trustee, and potentially Pravati. Include a certificate reflecting this.

### C. Timing and Sequencing Strategy

The brief's internal sequencing has problems:

**Lead with modification/subordination, not vacatur.** The brief leads with vacatur (Section I) and treats modification as alternative relief (Section III). This is backwards for procedural purposes:

- Vacatur is the more aggressive ask — it's more likely to be characterized as dispositive, more likely to trigger opposition, and harder to justify without an evidentiary hearing.
- Modification/subordination is a case-management measure that preserves BF's rights while allowing the case to proceed. It's squarely within inherent authority and clearly non-dispositive.
- Leading with modification signals reasonableness to the court and makes BF look obstructionist if it opposes.

**Recommendation:** Restructure. Lead with modification and subordination as the primary relief. Frame vacatur as the alternative if BF fails to substantiate within the court's deadline.

**Missing: motion for expedited briefing.** W.D. Tex. default briefing schedule gives the respondent 14 days to respond (LR CV-7(e)), then 7 days for reply. With the Magistrate's calendar, this motion won't be decided before May or June 2026 at earliest — leaving weeks before the July 23 discovery deadline. If the motion is treated as dispositive and goes to R&R, add another month for de novo review. The brief needs an accompanying motion to shorten time or set an expedited hearing.

**Missing: connection to specific discovery obligations.** The brief should identify what discovery tasks are blocked *right now* and what deadlines are approaching. For example:
- When are expert disclosures due?
- Are depositions scheduled that require travel funding?
- Are there document production obligations requiring contract attorneys?
- Has the court set a mediation deadline?

Tying the motion to a specific, imminent deadline (not just the general July 23 discovery close) creates procedural urgency.

### D. Waiver and Preservation Issues

**Timeliness of the motion.** BF filed its lien on March 5, 2024. The court ruled on intervention in Dkt. 329 (timeline unclear from brief, but appears to be mid-2024). It is now March 2026 — roughly two years after the lien was filed and perhaps 18+ months after the court's ruling on intervention. BF will argue that Plaintiffs sat on their rights. If the lien was improper from filing, why wait two years to move to vacate?

**Recommendation:** The brief needs to explain the delay. The strongest argument: Plaintiffs reasonably expected BF to conform its lien to the court's ruling voluntarily, and BF's continued refusal to quantify or subordinate despite repeated requests made this motion necessary only after co-counsel's withdrawal in November 2025 created a crisis. Pin the changed circumstances (co-counsel withdrawal, bankruptcy developments) as the trigger. Consider framing this as a "changed circumstances" motion rather than a challenge to the original lien filing.

**Preservation of the for-cause argument.** Section IV preserves the for-cause termination position. This is correct procedurally — don't waive it — but the current framing is potentially counterproductive. By raising it, the brief invites BF to litigate cause, which would require an evidentiary hearing and delay everything. 

**Recommendation:** Keep the reservation but make it crisper — one sentence, no factual detail. Remove the specific allegations about "failures in handling child custody matters and service of process" — those invite factual dispute. Simply state: "Plaintiffs preserve their position that BF was terminated for cause and reserve the right to seek a hearing on that issue if necessary."

### E. Opponent's Procedural Countermoves

Expect BF (or whoever responds) to:

1. **Challenge Magistrate authority** — argue this is dispositive, requiring R&R and de novo review. Delay of 2-3 months.
2. **Demand an evidentiary hearing** — argue that factual disputes (cause of termination, value of services, scope of work) preclude resolution on briefing alone.
3. **Move to compel arbitration** — if the engagement agreement has an arbitration clause, BF may argue the fee dispute (and thus the lien) must be resolved in arbitration, not by this court. The brief doesn't address this — **check the engagement agreement for an arbitration clause immediately.**
4. **Argue inadequate service/notice** — particularly given the bankruptcy complications.
5. **Cross-move for intervention again** — argue changed circumstances (need to defend lien) justify reconsideration of the intervention denial.
6. **Invoke the automatic stay** — if BF's bankruptcy is still open and the lien is estate property, vacating it may violate the § 362 automatic stay. Even if the trustee abandoned the entities, the stay may still apply to the individual debtor's interest. **This is potentially fatal if not addressed.**
7. **Argue Pravati's interest makes this motion incomplete** — if Pravati has an independent claim, vacating BF's lien doesn't solve the problem.

### F. Automatic Stay — The Unaddressed Elephant

The brief mentions BF's Chapter 7 bankruptcy but does not analyze whether the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. § 362(a) applies to this motion. If the lien is property of the bankruptcy estate, moving to vacate it may be an act "to obtain possession of property of the estate" or to "exercise control over property of the estate" — both prohibited by § 362(a)(3).

The brief says the trustee "abandoned" BF's firm entities. Under § 554, abandoned property reverts to the debtor. But:
- Was the lien specifically identified in the abandonment?
- Has the case been closed? (Abandonment under § 554(c) occurs at case closing.)
- If the lien was abandoned back to BF's principal, does the discharge injunction (§ 524) affect Plaintiffs' ability to challenge it?

**Recommendation:** This needs a clear statement. Either: (a) the automatic stay does not apply because the bankruptcy case has been closed and the lien was abandoned, with citation to the relevant bankruptcy docket entries; or (b) the motion is not an action against the debtor or estate property — it is an exercise of this court's inherent authority over its own docket, which the stay does not reach. *See In re Cano*, 410 B.R. 506 (Bankr. S.D. Tex. 2009) (police/regulatory exception). Get the bankruptcy docket and confirm the case status before filing.

### G. Local Rule Compliance — W.D. Tex. Specific

- **LR CV-7(a):** Motions must state the specific grounds and the relief sought. The brief does this adequately.
- **LR CV-7(i):** Certificate of conference required. **Missing from the brief.** Must be added.
- **LR CV-7(e):** Briefing page limits — motions limited to 20 pages. This brief may be close to or over the limit. Verify.
- **LR CV-7(f):** Appendix requirements for supporting evidence. The declarations referenced need to be filed as an appendix.
- **Standing Order of Magistrate Judge Chestney:** Check whether the Magistrate has a standing order with additional requirements for non-discovery motions (many W.D. Tex. magistrates do).
