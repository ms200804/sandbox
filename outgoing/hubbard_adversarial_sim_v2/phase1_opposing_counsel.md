# Opposing Counsel Analysis — BF's Perspective

**Calibration: Aggressive**

## Top 3 Weaknesses

1. **The brief conflates "law of the case" with adjudication of BF's lien rights** — The Court denied intervention; it did not adjudicate BF's lien. Dkt. 329 adopted Plaintiffs' opposition to intervention, not a ruling on the lien's validity. The brief repeatedly treats the intervention denial as a binding determination that BF has "no contingency interest" and is "limited to quantum meruit," but intervention and lien enforcement are distinct procedural vehicles with distinct standards. The Court never ruled on the lien itself. — **Severity: serious**

2. **Magistrate Judge authority is undersupported and potentially wrong on vacatur** — The brief waves away the dispositive question in a single paragraph. Vacating a charging lien—which is a recognized property interest under NY law—is arguably dispositive. The brief cites *Gomez v. United States* for a distinction between "ministerial" and "dispositive" functions, but *Gomez* involved jury selection, not extinguishment of property rights. If the Magistrate treats vacatur as dispositive, she can only issue an R&R, buying us months. — **Severity: serious**

3. **The brief assumes subordination is harmless to BF without proving it** — The proposed subordination structure puts BF behind the litigation funder AND replacement co-counsel. On a finite recovery, this could effectively eliminate BF's recovery entirely. The brief never addresses what happens if the recovery is modest—say $500K—and the funder takes its cut plus interest, co-counsel takes 33-40%, and there's nothing left. Calling this "preservation" of BF's interest is misleading when the practical effect may be extinguishment. — **Severity: serious**

## Strongest Single Attack Vector

**The lien is a vested property right that cannot be vacated or materially altered through a case-management motion without due process.**

I would lead with this because it reframes the entire motion. Plaintiffs characterize this as "docket management." It is not. A New York § 475 charging lien is a vested, enforceable property right that attaches from the commencement of the action. *LMWT Realty Corp. v. Davis Agency Inc.*, 85 N.Y.2d 462, 467 (1995). It cannot be destroyed through an ex parte or summary motion any more than a mechanic's lien can be stripped by a letter from the property owner. Vacating or materially subordinating this lien requires either (a) an adversary proceeding with full due process protections, or (b) a separate action. Plaintiffs' motion—filed as a "non-dispositive pretrial matter"—provides neither.

This attack works because it forces the Court to confront a threshold question before reaching the merits: *Does this procedural vehicle support this relief?* If the answer is no—or even maybe—the motion gets kicked to the District Judge or converted to a more formal proceeding, which delays the very relief Plaintiffs claim is urgent.

## Full Analysis

### A. The Intervention Ruling Is Not "Law of the Case" on the Lien

The brief's central rhetorical move is treating Dkt. 329 as a definitive ruling that BF is "limited to quantum meruit" and therefore the lien must be modified to reflect that limitation. This overstates what happened.

Dkt. 329 denied intervention. The Court adopted Plaintiffs' argument that a quantum meruit interest—as opposed to a contingency interest—does not support intervention under *Gaines v. Dixie Carriers*. That is a ruling about whether BF's interest is sufficient *to intervene as a party*. It is not a ruling about BF's substantive fee rights, the validity of the lien, or the enforceability of the engagement agreement.

Law of the case doctrine applies to issues "actually decided." *Agostini v. Felton*, 521 U.S. 203, 236 (1997). The intervention order decided that BF's claimed interest did not meet the *Gaines* standard. It did not decide:
- Whether BF is entitled to quantum meruit (it assumed it arguendo)
- The amount of any quantum meruit claim
- Whether the lien validly secures that claim
- Whether the lien should be subordinated

The brief's repeated assertion that the lien is "irreconcilable with the legal framework this Court has applied" is advocacy dressed as fact. The lien may overstate BF's interest—but that's an argument for modification of the lien's *description*, not vacatur of the lien itself.

**What I'd argue:** "The Court assumed for purposes of the intervention motion that BF's interest is quantum meruit. It did not adjudicate BF's fee rights. Plaintiffs now ask this Court to convert a procedural assumption into a substantive ruling—without briefing, without evidence, and without due process."

### B. The Magistrate Judge Lacks Authority to Vacate the Lien

The brief's treatment of Magistrate Judge authority is its weakest structural section. It devotes one paragraph to this issue, cites *Gomez* (inapposite), and asserts without analysis that "modification and subordination are pretrial case management."

A § 475 charging lien is a property interest. Stripping or materially subordinating it is dispositive. Under 28 U.S.C. § 636(b)(1)(A), a Magistrate Judge may not decide motions that are "dispositive of a claim or defense of a party." The Fifth Circuit takes a functional approach: if the practical effect of the order is to dispose of a claim, it's dispositive regardless of label. *See Castillo v. Frank*, 70 F.3d 382, 385 (5th Cir. 1995).

Even on the "modification and subordination" theory, placing BF's lien behind a litigation funder and new co-counsel on a case with uncertain recovery is functionally equivalent to extinguishment. That's dispositive.

**What I'd file:** A preliminary objection to the Magistrate Judge's authority, forcing the motion up to Judge Biery or at minimum converting it to an R&R process. This delays things—which benefits BF—and forces Plaintiffs to re-brief under a more demanding standard.

### C. Due Process — BF Is Entitled to More Than a Motion

BF's principal is in bankruptcy. The firm entities have been abandoned. The brief acknowledges serving the trustee and Pravati. But the brief does not address:

- Whether the trustee's abandonment means BF's principal personally holds the lien (which would make the bankruptcy estate irrelevant) or whether the lien passed through abandonment to the debtor
- Whether Pravati's claimed interest in the lien means Pravati is an indispensable party under Rule 19
- Whether BF's principal—who is in Chapter 7—has the resources or capacity to respond to this motion

Plaintiffs are asking the Court to strip a property right from a bankrupt individual through a case-management motion. At minimum, BF is entitled to an evidentiary hearing, discovery on the subordination terms, and an opportunity to present evidence on the quantum meruit value.

**What I'd file:** A motion for an evidentiary hearing, a Rule 19 motion to join Pravati as a necessary party, and a request for limited discovery on the litigation funding terms (which Plaintiffs have conspicuously not disclosed).

### D. The "Blocking" Narrative Is Plaintiffs' Problem, Not the Court's

The brief's emotional core is that BF's lien is "the sole obstacle" to Plaintiffs prosecuting their case. But this framing is misleading in several ways:

1. **Plaintiffs chose to fire BF.** The lien exists because Plaintiffs terminated the relationship. That is their right—but the consequence is that BF gets a lien. Plaintiffs cannot create the problem and then demand the Court eliminate it.

2. **The "blocking" is caused by the funder's requirements, not the lien.** Legalist requires a first-priority position. That is Legalist's business decision, not BF's fault. If Legalist won't fund with a senior lien in place, that's a negotiation problem, not a legal one. The Court should not strip BF's property right to accommodate a third-party funder's business model.

3. **Plaintiffs have other options.** The brief presents this as binary: subordinate BF's lien or the case dies. But Plaintiffs could (a) negotiate with BF on quantum meruit terms (not the impermissible contingency terms they rejected); (b) seek a different funder with different priority requirements; (c) seek pro bono assistance; (d) petition for appointment of counsel given the TVPA context. The brief forecloses these options without discussion.

4. **Current counsel chose to self-fund.** The brief cites $20,000 in discovery platform costs as evidence of hardship. A solo practitioner who takes on a multi-defendant TVPA case pro hac vice from California assumed this risk. That's not BF's problem.

### E. The § 1595(a) Fee-Shifting Argument Cuts Both Ways

The brief argues that the TVPA's mandatory fee-shifting provision means there will be "ample funds" to satisfy BF's claim. But:

- § 1595(a) fees are awarded to the *prevailing plaintiff's counsel*—i.e., current/future counsel. BF was discharged. It's not clear BF can claim a share of statutory fees at all.
- If the brief is right that § 1595(a) creates ample recovery, then BF's lien is *not* blocking the case—it just means BF will be paid from a different pot. The urgency narrative collapses.
- Plaintiffs cannot simultaneously argue "there will be plenty of money" and "BF's lien is destroying the case."

### F. Specific Line-Level Attacks

**"BF has refused to quantify its quantum meruit claim"** — This characterization assumes BF has an obligation to quantify pre-judgment. It does not. A § 475 lien attaches and is perfected by notice; quantification occurs at distribution. BF has no obligation to show its hand on valuation before there's a recovery to distribute.

**"The same type of arrangement BF itself previously pursued"** — If BF previously pursued Legalist funding, that was when BF *was counsel* and controlled the litigation. The fact that BF was willing to subordinate its own interests as lead counsel does not mean BF must accept subordination as a discharged former counsel with only a lien interest. Different posture, different calculus.

**"The firm entities were abandoned by the trustee"** — Abandonment under § 554 returns the property to the debtor. This means BF's principal *personally* holds the lien. This actually strengthens BF's position—it's not an orphaned asset, it's the personal property of an identifiable individual who can assert his rights.

**Judicial estoppel argument (NH v. Maine)** — The brief argues BF can't invoke Texas lien law because it filed under NY § 475-a. But judicial estoppel requires that the prior position was *accepted by the court*. The Court didn't rule on the lien notice at all. And in any event, BF can argue NY lien law while noting that Texas common law provides *independent* protection—belt and suspenders, not inconsistent positions.

### G. What I'd File

1. **Opposition to the motion** arguing: (a) the Magistrate lacks authority; (b) the lien is a property right requiring due process; (c) the intervention ruling is not law of the case on lien rights; (d) BF has no obligation to quantify pre-recovery; (e) Plaintiffs created this situation by firing BF.

2. **Preliminary objection** to Magistrate Judge authority, seeking referral to the District Judge.

3. **Rule 19 motion** to join Pravati as a necessary party (they claim an interest in the same recovery).

4. **Motion for evidentiary hearing** on the terms of the Legalist funding arrangement (Plaintiffs want the Court to subordinate BF's lien to this deal—the Court should see the deal).

5. **Discovery requests** for the Legalist term sheet, Nematzadeh's proposed fee arrangement, and current counsel's fee agreement. If BF is being subordinated to these interests, BF is entitled to know what they are.

### H. Sections That Are Well-Constructed

**Section II (facial inconsistency) is well-constructed and I have no material concerns.** The argument that the lien's face text claims a contingency percentage rather than a quantum meruit amount is factually accurate and logically sound. I can quibble with the *consequence* (modification vs. vacatur), but the observation itself is correct. The lien notice does claim 37.5% and does not state a dollar figure.

**The procedural history section is clean.** The chronology is accurate, well-sourced to docket entries, and doesn't overstate what the Court decided. My objections go to the *legal conclusions* drawn from this history, not the history itself.

---

## Summary Assessment

This brief is competently drafted and makes the best of a strong factual position (BF really has been unresponsive for two years, and the lien really is blocking progress). But it has three exploitable structural weaknesses: (1) it overstates the intervention ruling as a definitive adjudication of BF's rights, (2) it undersupports the Magistrate Judge's authority to grant this relief, and (3) it treats subordination as harmless without confronting the possibility that it functionally extinguishes BF's interest. An aggressive opposition can delay this motion significantly by challenging jurisdiction, demanding due process protections, and forcing joinder of Pravati—buying time and forcing Plaintiffs to re-file or re-brief under less favorable conditions.
