### ARGUMENT SECTION III — AUTHORITY (New Section)

This section collects the legal authority arguments that were previously scattered across the Legal Standard and Section I:

> ### III. THE COURT HAS AUTHORITY TO GRANT THIS RELIEF
>
> Federal courts possess inherent authority to manage their proceedings, including the power to control their dockets and ensure the orderly prosecution of cases. *Chambers v. NASCO, Inc.*, 501 U.S. 32, 43 (1991). This authority is not limited to sanctioning bad-faith litigation conduct — it encompasses "the power to fashion an appropriate sanction for conduct which abuses the judicial process" and, more broadly, to manage the incidents of litigation pending before the court. *Id.* at 44–45.
>
> A charging lien is an incident of the litigation. When it obstructs the case it was meant to protect, the Court has authority to address it. The Fifth Circuit has recognized that federal courts may determine lien priority and order allocation of recovery among competing claimants. *Villanueva v. CNA Ins. Cos.*, 868 F.2d 684, 686–87 (5th Cir. 1989); *Speaks v. Trikora Lloyd P.T.*, 838 F.2d 1436, 1440 (5th Cir. 1988). Those cases addressed priority at the point of distribution. The same authority must extend to a lien that is preventing the case from reaching distribution — the need for judicial intervention is more acute, not less, when the lien is killing the litigation. No Fifth Circuit case has addressed this precise posture, but the principle follows from the recognized authority to manage liens as an incident of docket control. *Chambers*, 501 U.S. at 43–44.
>
> Independently, New York law provides the substantive framework. A § 475 charging lien is equitable in nature. *Schneider*, 302 A.D.2d at 187–88. Its enforcement is "founded upon the equitable notion that the proceeds of a settlement are ultimately under the control of the court." *Id.* Lien priority "depend[s] on the equities." *Banque Indosuez*, 98 N.Y.2d at 43–44. A court may decline to enforce a lien — or modify its terms — where the equities require it. *Id.*; *Rosenman & Colin v. Richard*, 850 F.2d 57, 60 (2d Cir. 1988).
>
> The equities here are plain. BF holds a lien claiming an interest that governing law does not permit, has refused to quantify a claim it has had two years to substantiate, and is blocking the prosecution of a trafficking case by two victims who had no voice in BF's decision to maintain an unquantified lien on their case. The Court has both inherent authority and equitable authority under governing New York law to grant the requested relief.

Upon abandonment, property reverts to the debtor and the automatic stay ceases to apply. 11 U.S.C. § 554(a); *In re Zaptocky*, 250 F.3d 1020, 1027 (6th Cir. 2001); *In re Dewsnup*, 908 F.2d 588, 590 (10th Cir. 1990), *aff'd on other grounds*, 502 U.S. 410 (1992). >
> [Alternative — if the abandonment notice did not specifically address the lien:] The trustee abandoned BF's firm entities — the only entities that could assert the lien — as having no value to the estate. >

The stay prohibits "any act to obtain possession of property of the estate or of property from the estate or to exercise control over property of the estate." 11 U.S.C. § 362(a)(3). Modifying the lien's terms to conform to governing law and adjusting its priority do not transfer possession or divest the estate of property. ## Unfixable Issues

1. **No binding Fifth Circuit authority for pre-judgment lien modification.** If no case exists in which a Fifth Circuit court modified a charging lien as pre-judgment case management, the brief cannot manufacture one. The inherent-authority + NY-equity framework is the best available path. The revision acknowledges the gap candidly rather than overstating existing authority.

2. **Subordination may functionally extinguish BF's interest on a modest recovery.** If the case settles for a small amount, the funder and co-counsel take first and second position, and BF gets nothing. The § 1595(a) fee-shifting argument mitigates this (statutory fees are an additional source), but on a small recovery it remains a real problem. The brief cannot guarantee BF gets paid.

3. **The "case management, not fee adjudication" framing has a logical tension.** The motion necessarily argues that BF's lien claims an interest it isn't entitled to — which is, at some level, a statement about BF's fee rights. The revised brief draws the line as clearly as possible (we're conforming the lien to agreed-upon law, not determining what BF is owed), but a sophisticated judge will see the tension. The best mitigation is the for-cause cut — removing that sentence eliminates the most glaring inconsistency.

4. **BF's due process argument has real force.** BF was denied intervention — meaning it has no procedural vehicle to litigate its fee rights in this action. If the Court modifies the lien, BF will argue it was deprived of property without due process. The brief's answer (BF has notice and can be heard on this motion; quantum meruit rights are preserved; fee amount is for a separate proceeding) is adequate but not airtight. The judge may want to give BF formal opportunity to respond, which could slow the timeline.
