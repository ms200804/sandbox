# Procedural Specialist Analysis

## Top 3 Weaknesses

1. **Magistrate Judge authority argument is underdeveloped and vulnerable** — If BF argues vacatur is dispositive, the Magistrate Judge may lack authority to enter final relief under § 636(b)(1)(A), and the brief's fallback is a single parenthetical cite to *Gomez*. An adverse ruling on authority wastes months. — **Severity: serious**

2. **No Local Rule compliance discussion** — The brief does not address W.D. Tex. Local Rules (particularly LR CV-7, the meet-and-confer requirement, page limits, or whether this motion requires a separate briefing schedule). If counsel did not confer with BF before filing, the motion is subject to summary denial. — **Severity: serious**

3. **Bankruptcy stay analysis is conclusory** — The brief asserts in two sentences that the automatic stay doesn't apply because the trustee abandoned the entities. But the lien itself may be estate property that wasn't abandoned (the trustee abandoned the *entities*, not necessarily the *lien claim*). If the stay applies, the motion is void ab initio. — **Severity: fatal if wrong**

## Strongest Single Concern

**The bankruptcy stay issue.** If BF's charging lien is property of the bankruptcy estate — distinct from the abandoned firm entities — then this motion may violate the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. § 362(a). The brief conflates abandonment of the firm entities with abandonment of the lien. A charging lien is a chose in action with potential value; a trustee who abandons a law firm as non-viable might still retain the lien as an asset. If the stay applies, every order entered on this motion is void, not merely voidable. This needs to be bulletproofed before filing.

## Full Analysis

### A. Magistrate Judge Authority (§ 636 Issue)

The brief correctly identifies that this motion is before a Magistrate Judge on pretrial referral, not consent jurisdiction. But the analysis of the MJ's authority is thin and reactive rather than proactive.

**The problem:** Under § 636(b)(1)(A), a Magistrate Judge may decide non-dispositive pretrial matters. The exclusion list includes "a motion… to dismiss or to permit maintenance of a class action, [or] to involuntarily dismiss an action." If BF argues that vacating a lien extinguishes a property right and is therefore dispositive, the MJ might conclude she can only issue a Report and Recommendation under § 636(b)(1)(B), adding months of delay.

**What the brief does:** It offers one paragraph asserting this is "pretrial case management" with a single parenthetical cite to *Gomez v. United States* — a case about magistrate judges presiding over jury selection, not lien management. That's a poor fit.

**What it should do:**
- Lead with the modify/subordinate relief (which is clearly non-dispositive — it preserves BF's interest) and position vacatur as the alternative that may require R&R treatment. The brief already does this structurally but doesn't connect it to the § 636 analysis.
- Cite *Flam v. Flam*, 788 F.3d 1043 (9th Cir. 2015), or equivalent Fifth Circuit authority on what constitutes "dispositive" — the test is whether the motion "has the effect of dismissing a claim." Modification doesn't.
- Add: "Even if the Court determines that vacatur requires a Report and Recommendation, the primary relief sought — modification and subordination — is non-dispositive and may be entered directly."
- Consider citing the specific referral order (Dkt. 429) and its scope language.

**Section is currently: needs strengthening.** The structure is right but the authority is weak.

### B. Local Rule Compliance (W.D. Tex.)

This is an omission, not a drafting error, but it's the kind of thing that gets motions summarily denied.

**Meet and confer:** W.D. Tex. LR CV-7(i) requires that before filing most non-dispositive motions, the movant must confer (or attempt to confer) with opposing counsel and state in the motion whether the relief is opposed. The brief does not contain a meet-and-confer certification.

**Specific issues to address:**
- Has counsel attempted to confer with BF's principal (who is pro se in bankruptcy and may or may not still be authorized to act for BF)?
- Has counsel conferred with the Chapter 7 trustee, who may be the real party in interest?
- If conferral was impossible (BF is effectively defunct, principal is bankrupt), the brief needs to say so explicitly and explain the efforts made.

**Certificate of conference** should appear early in the motion, not buried. Its absence is the kind of defect that Judge Chestney's chambers may flag before even reading the substance.

**Page limits:** Under LR CV-7(d), briefs in support of motions are limited to 25 pages unless leave is granted. This brief appears to be at or near that limit. Verify compliance.

### C. The Bankruptcy Stay Problem

This is the motion's most dangerous procedural vulnerability. The brief's analysis:

> "The trustee has abandoned BF's firm entities as having no viable assets. Upon abandonment, the automatic stay under 11 U.S.C. § 362(a) ceases to apply to abandoned property."

**The logical gap:** The trustee abandoned the *firm entities* (Balestriere PLLC and Balestriere Fariello). But did the trustee abandon the *charging lien in this case*? These are different assets. A law firm with no viable operations can still hold a valuable chose in action — a charging lien on a TVPA case with potential for substantial recovery. The trustee's abandonment of the entities does not automatically encompass abandonment of the lien itself.

**What needs to happen before filing:**
1. Obtain documentation of exactly what the trustee abandoned — the abandonment notice or order from the bankruptcy court.
2. If the lien was not specifically abandoned, consider: (a) filing a motion in the bankruptcy court for relief from stay under § 362(d); or (b) contacting the trustee to confirm the lien is considered abandoned or to request abandonment under § 554(b).
3. If the trustee *did* abandon the lien, cite the specific abandonment order. If the trustee abandoned the entities and the lien was an asset of those entities, make that chain of reasoning explicit with authority.

**Risk if this is wrong:** Actions taken in violation of the automatic stay are void (not voidable) in the Fifth Circuit. *Kalb v. Feuerstein*, 308 U.S. 433 (1940); *In re Soares*, 107 F.3d 969 (1st Cir. 1997). If the stay applies and Plaintiffs obtain an order modifying the lien, BF (or the trustee) can have it vacated at any time. Worse, it signals to the court that counsel didn't do the bankruptcy homework.

**The Pravati wrinkle:** The brief mentions Pravati Capital "has asserted its own lien against BF" and "claimed entitlement to a percentage of any recovery in this case." If Pravati has a security interest in BF's accounts receivable or fee rights, Pravati may be a necessary party to any lien modification. Has Pravati been given notice? The Certificate of Service says yes — good. But has Pravati's interest been addressed substantively? If Pravati holds a perfected security interest in BF's lien, subordinating BF's lien also subordinates Pravati's interest. Pravati will likely oppose.

### D. Timing and Sequencing

**Discovery deadline pressure:** The brief correctly identifies July 23, 2026 as the discovery deadline. Filing today (March 2026) gives approximately four months. That's tight but workable *if* the motion is decided quickly.

**Recommended sequence:**
1. File motion (now)
2. BF has 21 days to respond (LR CV-7(e))
3. Plaintiffs have 14 days to reply
4. If hearing requested: add 2-4 weeks for scheduling
5. If MJ decides it's dispositive and issues R&R: add 14 days for objections, then weeks/months for District Judge review

**Total realistic timeline:** 2-4 months if non-dispositive; 3-6 months if treated as dispositive. The discovery deadline is 4 months away. If treated as dispositive, this motion may not be resolved before the discovery deadline.

**What the brief should add:** A request for expedited consideration. The brief asks for "oral argument at the Court's earliest convenience" but does not formally move for expedited briefing or an expedited hearing. Given the discovery deadline, counsel should file a separate motion for expedited consideration or at minimum add a section explaining the urgency and requesting shortened response times.

### E. Service and Notice Issues

The Certificate of Service lists service on: (1) BF's principal, (2) the Chapter 7 trustee, and (3) Pravati Capital. This is good — these are the interested parties.

**But:**
- Is BF's principal authorized to respond on behalf of BF? If the firm entities were abandoned by the trustee, they reverted to the debtor. But a PLLC in New York requires an attorney in good standing. If Balestriere's law license is suspended or he's not in good standing, who speaks for the abandoned entity?
- Has the bankruptcy trustee been served in his capacity as trustee, or personally? The form matters.
- Service "by certified mail / email" — pick one and state it. The bracketed alternatives suggest this is still a draft. Don't file it with brackets.

### F. Choice of Law / Forum Issues

The brief relies heavily on NY lien law (§ 475) and Second Circuit authority. This is appropriate given the choice-of-law concession and the Court's prior ruling. But there's a procedural nuance worth flagging:

**The Court adopted Plaintiffs' opposition "for the reasons stated."** This is law of the case on the intervention issue. But the intervention ruling addressed whether BF had a sufficient interest to intervene — it did not directly rule on the validity or scope of the charging lien. BF will argue that the intervention ruling doesn't control the lien analysis. The brief should be more precise about what exactly is law of the case (quantum meruit limitation) versus what must be independently established (authority to modify the lien).

**Section II's treatment of this is sound** — it correctly frames the lien as "facially inconsistent" with the Court's framework. This section is well-constructed and I have no material concerns with its legal reasoning.

### G. Preservation for Appeal

**Waiver risks:** The brief correctly preserves the for-cause termination argument ("Plaintiffs preserve their position that BF was terminated for cause"). This is important — if Plaintiffs win on modification/subordination, they don't want to be deemed to have waived the for-cause argument for the fee dispute itself.

**What else to preserve:** The brief should explicitly note that Plaintiffs do not waive any claims against BF (malpractice, breach of fiduciary duty, etc.) by seeking modification rather than vacatur. The language "release all claims against BF" in the factual section describes what BF demanded — but the brief should affirmatively state that this motion is without prejudice to Plaintiffs' independent claims.

### H. Opponent's Procedural Countermoves

BF (or the trustee, or Pravati) will likely:

1. **Move for extension of time to respond** — BF's principal is bankrupt, may be pro se, and may argue he needs time to obtain counsel for the entities. *The brief should preemptively address this by noting that BF has had two years of notice.*
2. **Argue the automatic stay bars this motion** — As discussed above, this is the strongest procedural attack.
3. **Argue this is dispositive and requires an adversary proceeding** — BF could analogize to bankruptcy adversary proceedings and argue that modifying a property right requires full due process protections, not a simple motion.
4. **File a cross-motion to enforce the lien** — BF might argue the lien is valid as filed and move to enforce it, or move to intervene again on different grounds.
5. **Argue Pravati is a necessary party under Rule 19** — If Pravati holds a security interest in the lien proceeds, BF could argue the motion cannot proceed without Pravati's formal joinder.
6. **Move to transfer the lien dispute to SDNY** — Where the fee agreement was formed, where the bankruptcy is pending, and where NY lien law is home-court law.

### I. The Judicial Estoppel Argument

The brief invokes *New Hampshire v. Maine* for the proposition that BF cannot pivot from NY law (under which it filed its lien) to Texas law. **This section is sound.** BF filed under § 475-a, the Court applied NY law, and BF conceded NY law governs. Judicial estoppel is properly invoked.

### J. Section III (Modification and Subordination)

**The 30-day deadline for time records** is a smart procedural mechanism — it shifts the burden to BF and creates a self-executing vacatur trigger. But:

- Is 30 days reasonable given that BF's principal is bankrupt and the firm may lack records? If the court thinks 30 days is too short, it may impose 60 or 90 — which eats into the discovery timeline. Consider proposing 30 days but noting willingness to accept a reasonable alternative.
- The brief doesn't specify what form the documentation must take. "Contemporaneous time records" is the gold standard, but if BF never kept time records (plausible for a contingency-fee firm), there may be nothing to produce. The brief should address this: if no records exist, then BF cannot substantiate a quantum meruit claim, and the lien should be vacated.

**Section III is generally well-constructed** and represents the strongest part of the motion from a procedural standpoint. The tiered relief structure (modify → substantiate → subordinate → vacate if noncompliant) is procedurally sound.
