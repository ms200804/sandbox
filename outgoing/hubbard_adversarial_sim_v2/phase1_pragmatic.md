# Pragmatic Review — Hubbard v. Crow, Motion to Modify/Subordinate Charging Lien

---

## Top 3 Weaknesses

1. **The relief structure is inverted — you're burying the ask that gets you what you need.** The title says "Modify and Subordinate... or in the Alternative, Vacate," and the argument leads with vacatur rhetoric before getting to modification. But what you *actually need* is subordination to a funder and co-counsel. A magistrate judge will be far more comfortable entering a narrow, preservation-oriented order than vacating a property interest. Lead with the scalpel, not the sledgehammer. — **Severity: serious**

2. **The brief reads long for what it's asking.** This is a case-management motion before a magistrate judge. You're asking for three things: cap the lien to quantum meruit, make them show time records in 30 days, and subordinate to funder/co-counsel. That's a 10-page motion. This is closer to 25 pages of substance. The repetition — "irreconcilable with the legal framework this Court has applied" appears verbatim at least five times — dilutes impact and signals insecurity about the argument. A magistrate with a full docket will appreciate concision. — **Severity: serious**

3. **The Pravati Capital / bankruptcy subplot is introduced but never resolved.** You raise that Pravati claims an independent percentage of recovery, that BF's principal is bankrupt, that the trustee abandoned the entities, and that it's unclear who would even receive lien proceeds. These are powerful facts. But you don't connect them to your requested relief in any structured way. You gesture at "unresolved questions" but don't explain why those questions support modification now. Either build that into the argument or cut it to a single footnote. As written, it creates confusion about standing and competing interests without paying off. — **Severity: minor-to-serious** (it's a missed opportunity that also muddies the narrative)

## Strongest Single Concern

**The brief is arguing the merits of the fee dispute while insisting it isn't.** You repeatedly say "this motion does not ask the Court to adjudicate the underlying fee dispute," but then spend substantial ink arguing that BF's contingency claim is "irreconcilable" with governing law, that BF may have been terminated for cause (forfeiting all fees), and that BF may not even have time records. The brief can't have it both ways.

This matters because it gives BF a clean procedural objection: "They say they're not asking for fee adjudication, but that's exactly what they're doing — they want the Court to rule that our lien overstates our entitlement, cap it at quantum meruit, and force us to prove up in 30 days. That *is* a fee adjudication, and it requires an adversary proceeding with proper notice and an opportunity to be heard, not a pretrial motion."

**Fix:** Sharpen the distinction. The Court has *already* ruled on the legal framework (Dkt. 329). You're not asking for a new ruling — you're asking the Court to conform the docket to its own prior order. The modification isn't adjudicating the fee; it's requiring the lien to reflect established law of the case. Frame it as ministerial/conforming, not adjudicative. And drop the "terminated for cause" preservation — it undercuts the "we're not litigating the fee" posture. Save it for the fee proceeding itself.

## Filing Recommendations

**Cut or condense:**
- The Preliminary Statement should be cut by 40%. It's a full brief summary. For a magistrate judge, your first paragraph should be: trafficking victims, former counsel has unquantified lien for two years, lien blocks funding and co-counsel, case will miss deadlines. Second paragraph: what you're asking for. Done.
- Section II (lien is facially inconsistent) is almost entirely redundant with Section I. Fold the key point — the lien claims 37.5% contingency, the Court has ruled BF is limited to quantum meruit — into Section I as a sub-paragraph. Cut Section II as a standalone.
- The phrase "irreconcilable with the legal framework this Court has applied" should appear once, maybe twice. Not five-plus times. It reads like a brief that doesn't trust its own argument.

**Move:**
- Section III (the actual requested relief — modify and subordinate) should be Section I. Lead with what you want. The judge should know within the first two pages exactly what order you're requesting. *Then* explain why the Court has authority and why the facts support it.
- The Magistrate Judge Authority section is correctly placed but slightly over-argued. The parenthetical on *Gomez* is unnecessary — that case is about jury trial rights in misdemeanor cases, not lien management. If opposing counsel challenges magistrate authority, you can brief it more fully in reply. For now, one paragraph citing § 636(b)(1)(A) and the referral order is sufficient.

**Rewrite:**
- The Conclusion's requested relief is well-structured but should mirror the argument headings. Right now the argument goes: (I) lien is obstructing, (II) lien is inconsistent, (III) modify and subordinate. The conclusion goes: (1) modify, (2) vacate. The mismatch makes it harder to track. If you restructure as I recommend (lead with the requested relief), this resolves itself.
- "Plaintiffs preserve their position that BF was terminated for cause and is entitled to no compensation under New York law." — **Cut this.** It's a landmine. You've just spent pages telling the judge you're *not* asking for fee adjudication. This sentence invites the judge to think "well, if they're preserving a total-forfeiture argument, maybe this *is* a fee dispute dressed up as case management." Preserve it in a separate letter or in the fee proceeding. Not here.

**Add:**
- A proposed order. Always. You're asking a magistrate judge to enter a three-part order. Draft it for her. Judges enter proposed orders far more readily than they draft their own from a brief's conclusion.
- One clean paragraph on the TVPA fee-shifting point. You mention § 1595(a) in passing in Section I. It deserves more emphasis — it's your strongest equitable argument for subordination. The statute *guarantees* fees to prevailing plaintiffs. That means subordination doesn't risk BF getting nothing; it means BF gets paid *if and only if* the case succeeds, which it can't do with the lien in place. This is the "your honor, everyone wins" argument. Put it up front.

## Full Analysis

### Preliminary Statement
The narrative is effective — trafficking victims, two years of obstruction, solo practitioner, deadlines approaching. The emotional and practical urgency comes through. But it's too long. A preliminary statement for a case-management motion should be one page, not two. The specific details about BF's refusal to subordinate, the bankruptcy, and the specific terms demanded belong in the Statement of Facts, not the intro. The judge needs the headline, not the full story, in the first section.

The final paragraph ("Plaintiffs do not ask the Court to adjudicate the underlying fee dispute") is the right framing and should stay — but only if you follow through on it consistently, which the brief currently does not.

### Procedural History
Well-constructed. The chronological narrative is clean, the docket citations are specific, and the New York law choice-of-law point is established early. Two notes:

- The parenthetical "(BF's own plea conceding same)" at Dkt. 318 at 5 is good — keep it.
- "BF disputes the 'for cause' characterization; that issue is not before the Court on this motion." — This is well-handled. Clean acknowledgment without concession. But then you undermine it at the end of Section III by "preserving" the for-cause argument. Pick one posture.

The paragraph on the bankruptcy filing and Pravati is fine here as procedural background. Just don't re-introduce it later without connecting it to the argument.

### Statement of Facts

**Section A (Lien Blocking Prosecution):** This is the heart of your motion and it works. The Legalist relationship, the co-counsel withdrawal, the solo practitioner funding $20K out of pocket, the Nematzadeh declaration — all strong. The Nematzadeh credentials paragraph is appropriate; a judge wants to know that replacement counsel is serious, not ambulance-chasing.

One flag: "the same type of arrangement that BF itself had previously pursued with this same funder while BF was still counsel of record" — this is a strong fact but you use it three times across the brief. Once in the facts, once more at most in the argument. Repetition weakens it.

**Section B (Refusal to Quantify):** Sound. The factual predicate is established cleanly: two years, no time records, no dollar figure. The hint that records "may not exist" is well-placed — it's devastating without being accusatory.

**Section C (Bankruptcy):** As noted above, this introduces facts that aren't fully connected to the argument. The "unresolved questions about who would receive the proceeds" framing is too passive. Either argue that this uncertainty itself supports subordination (because the lien isn't protecting any identifiable party's interest) or trim this to background.

### Magistrate Judge Authority
Adequate but slightly over-argued for the motion itself. The *Gomez* citation is a stretch — that case involves the constitutional limits on magistrate judge authority in criminal trials. It's not wrong to cite for the general proposition, but it's the kind of cite that draws attention to the question of whether this is *really* non-dispositive. A confident brief cites § 636(b)(1)(A), notes the referral order, and moves on. If BF challenges authority, brief it fully in reply.

The final sentence — "This motion falls squarely within that referral" — is the right close. The preceding paragraph could be cut by half.

### Legal Standard
This section is well-organized and the authorities are correctly marshaled. *Chambers*, *Villanueva*, *Cooperman*, *Schneider*, *Banque Indosuez* — these are the right cases. The judicial estoppel point on BF invoking § 475-a is clever and well-placed.

One concern: The *New Hampshire v. Maine* cite for judicial estoppel is technically correct but potentially disproportionate — it's a Supreme Court sovereign-immunity case. A New York or Second Circuit judicial estoppel case would be more natural here. If you have one, swap it in. If not, it's fine but slightly flashy for a case-management motion.

### Argument Section I (Lien Is Obstructing)
This is where the brief is strongest as advocacy. The *Butler* cite on "litigation impasse" is perfect — it's directly on point and from the Second Circuit. The *a fortiori* argument (if a court can rearrange priority at distribution, it can address a lien preventing distribution) is logical and well-framed.

The bulleted pattern-of-conduct section is effective. However, it reads slightly hot — "Nothing" as a standalone sentence after the refusal-to-quantify bullet is the kind of rhetorical punctuation that works in oral argument but reads as editorializing on paper, especially before a magistrate judge. Consider cutting that single word.

The TVPA fee-shifting paragraph buried near the end of this section is your best equitable argument and should be promoted. "If Plaintiffs prevail, there will be ample funds from which to satisfy a reasonable quantum meruit claim — including the statutory fee award. If the lien prevents Plaintiffs from prosecuting their case, the lien itself becomes worthless." That's the money line. It should be in the first two pages.

### Argument Section II (Facially Inconsistent)
This section is redundant. Every point it makes has already been made in the Legal Standard and Section I. The law-of-the-case argument is important, but it can be stated in two sentences within Section I rather than occupying its own section heading. Cut this section entirely and redistribute its one unique point (the *Cooperman*/*Lai Ling Cheng* framework as applied to the specific 37.5% claim) into Section I or III.

### Argument Section III (Modify and Subordinate)
This is well-structured as to the three-part relief requested. The "First... Second... Third" framework is clear, actionable, and gives the judge a roadmap.

The 30-day deadline for time records is aggressive but defensible given two years of refusal. Expect BF to argue it's too short, especially given the bankruptcy. Consider whether 45 or 60 days would cost you anything strategically — if the real goal is subordination to the funder, the deadline for records is secondary. You don't want the judge to deny the whole motion because one component feels rushed.

The bankruptcy stay paragraph is necessary and correctly analyzed. The distinction between modification/subordination and extinguishment is sound. Keep this.

**"Plaintiffs preserve their position that BF was terminated for cause..."** — As discussed above, cut this. It's the single most damaging sentence in the brief to your own framing.

### Conclusion
The requested relief is clearly stated. Add a proposed order. Request a hearing (which you do). The "such other and further relief" catchall is standard and fine.

### Tone
The tone is mostly right — firm, professional, and appropriately urgent. A few places push toward indignation ("Nothing," the repeated "irreconcilable" formulation) that you should sand down. This is a motion before a magistrate judge, not a jury. Magistrate judges respond to practical problem-solving. The strongest version of this brief says: "Here's a mess on your docket, here's a clean three-part order that fixes it, everyone's interests are protected, and we can get back to litigating the actual case." That's 80% of what this brief does. Remove the 20% that sounds like you're relitigating BF's conduct.

### Overall Assessment
This is a solid brief with a strong factual predicate and sound legal framework. The weaknesses are structural (argument order, redundancy, length) and tonal (edge of relitigating the fee dispute while claiming not to), not substantive. The law of the case is favorable. The equities are overwhelmingly in your favor. A tighter, shorter version of this brief — leading with the requested relief, cutting Section II, promoting the TVPA fee-shifting argument, and attaching a proposed order — would be more effective. I would sign it after those revisions.
